# Backhand Checkpoints

Tyler's 5-checkpoint framework for the backhand, introduced in the Feb 2026 masterclass. The same five checkpoints apply to backhand drives, drops, dinks, and volleys — only the swing path at step 4 (extension) changes by shot type.

## The Five Checkpoints

### Checkpoint 1: Take-Back
**Position:** Butt cap of the paddle points toward the ball; paddle face sideways (visible from the side); paddle tip pointing up AND back diagonally.

Not straight up. Not down. The tip goes up-and-back. This creates the leverage needed for the swing and cannot be skipped.

**Visual test:** From the side, you should be able to see the paddle face. If you can't, the take-back is not complete.

**Cue:** "Put the car in drive before hitting the gas." Body sideways + paddle back = car in drive. Body forward, no setup = neutral.

**From the May 2026 video:** Two checkpoints for the turn: (1) paddle tip pointing back toward the back fence or people behind you; (2) hips pointing directly to the side.

### Checkpoint 2: The Drop
**Position:** After take-back (butt cap forward, tip up), let the tip fall from up-to-down. Butt cap shifts from forward to pointing upward.

**How:** Release the death grip. "Pretend there's a weight on the paddle tip." Let gravity do this — don't force it down.

**Why:** This drop gets you under the ball so you can swing upward rather than pushing into it. Skipping this step produces a weak, armsy backhand with no power.

**The C-with-check-mark shape:** Back = top of C; drop = curve of C; forward extension = horizontal check mark; follow-through = upward finish.

### Checkpoint 3: Contact
**Position:** Out in front AND away from the body. "Leave room for the Holy Ghost."

Distance between paddle and body at take-back creates distance at contact. Contact too close to the body = guaranteed pop-up. Contact out front and away = power and control.

**Common failure:** Players think they've done checkpoint 1 correctly but their body is still too square, collapsing the contact space.

### Checkpoint 4: Extension
**What Tyler calls "the secret ingredient."** 80% of players who have decent take-back, drop, and contact still miss this step.

After contact, keep the paddle swinging forward — do NOT follow through over the shoulder immediately. Point the paddle tip directly toward the target before the shoulder rotation takes over.

**Cue:** "Hit through three balls." The first ball is where you were; the contact is the second; the extension goes through a third imaginary ball.

**How swing path changes by shot:**
- **Drive:** Swing forward (ball penetrates through the court)
- **Drop:** Swing upward (ball falls into the kitchen)
- **Dink:** Short forward push (compact, shoulder-driven)
- Both drive and drop share the same prep — only extension direction differs

### Checkpoint 5: Follow-Through
The natural result of steps 1–4. Do not force it.

**Position:** Paddle touches the back, elbows point up, frame of paddle tip pointing up. "Smell your armpits." Wherever the elbows point, the ball went. Hold the pose — "pretend you're on center court, smile for cameras."

**The hold matters:** Finishing and holding reinforces the full swing path in muscle memory.

## The "Top Hand Does the Talking" Rule

For a right-handed player, the top (left) hand drives the backhand stroke. As skill level increases, the bottom hand contributes more — but beginners must learn top-hand dominance first or the stroke stays weak and mechanical.

## One Hand vs. Two Hands

Tyler's preference: **drives with two hands, drops with one hand.** Two-handed drives give more stability and power. One-handed drops give more touch and the ability to open the face. Two-handed roll drops (the "drip") are also valid.

General rule: "Stick with what you have. You're always going to want the other hand — don't switch after years of developing your current backhand."

## Compact at Higher Levels

The checkpoints apply at full, deliberate scale for beginners. As skill increases, compress everything: shorter take-back, drop happens in a flash, contact quicker. Start big to learn the sequence, then tighten.

## The Biggest Mistake: Avoiding Backhands

From the May 2026 video: "The only reason you can't improve is because you don't actually hit backhands." Players run around backhands, never develop them, and blame the shot. The fix is simpler than any technical cue — commit to hitting it.
