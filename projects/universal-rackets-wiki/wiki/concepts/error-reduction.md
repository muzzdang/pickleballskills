# Error Reduction

Tyler's overarching philosophy for club-level play: **you don't need to hit more winners — you need to make fewer errors**. The pickleball ball is designed to come back. At club level, the team that misses less wins. Stop trying to end points; make opponents hit more balls.

## The Core Math

An in-ball (even if not great) gives the opponent a chance to miss. A ball hit out is 100% your opponent's point. A 50/50 shot when the downside is the point is never a good gamble.

"You live to play another point." Even a popped-up ball may not be put away. Stay in the point, defend, and get back into position. See [Reset and Defense](../concepts/reset-and-defense.md).

## Drives: 60–70%

The 100% drive looks impressive but fails the majority of the time. A 60–70% drive produces the same result (short, weak reply) with far higher consistency. Tyler and Michele demonstrate live: hitting 60% drive causes Michele to miss. 100% drive is not demonstrated because the point is already made.

**The drill:** Stand at the baseline with a partner. Hit drives back and forth at 60%. This is the drill Tyler credits for fixing his own drive after transitioning from tennis.

This is consistent across every serve, drive, and 3rd shot video in the channel. There is no exception made for aggressive situations — even when attacking, the margin-first mindset means aiming inside target zones, not at lines.

## Drops: Height Over Depth

Being afraid of the pop-up leads to hitting into the net — which is 100% a loss. A slightly high drop gives the opponent a chance to miss. "Over the net's better than in the net."

**The drill:** 10 consecutive drops into the kitchen — height allowed, net not allowed. If it goes in the net, start over.

The goal of the drop is not a perfect, low, unattackable ball on every attempt. The goal is: consistently in the kitchen, within reach of the net post, and low enough not to be hammered. Perfection is the enemy of consistency.

## Volleys: Placement Over Power, Multiple Shots

Do not try to end the point with one volley. Hit the first with high margin, then adjust. Move the ball to the opponent's backhand, then move them again. A volley that is in and on target is almost always better than a volley that goes for the line.

## Dinks: The Push

Open the paddle face and push forward. Don't say "don't pop it up" — focus on the action (forward push). The mechanical focus replaces the negative internal instruction. See [Kitchen Dominance](../concepts/kitchen-dominance.md).

## Placement Beats Power Every Time

"Put the ball where your opponent isn't — makes them look like they're chasing a headless chicken. No special shots required."

**Placement strategies from the Apr/May 2026 videos:**
- **Side-to-side dinking:** Left-foot/right-foot, move opponents until they pop a ball up. Patient construction, not brute force.
- **Target the transition zone:** Aim at the feet of whoever is still in no man's land (furthest back player in doubles). In doubles, always hit to the person furthest back.
- **Hit behind moving opponents:** Good players follow the ball. Aim where they're moving away from. A ball aimed behind a fast-moving opponent catches them off-balance.
- **Court angles open after center:** Going for wide angles before establishing the middle produces errors. Set up the middle, earn the angle.

## Shot Selection by Contact Height

The error-reduction framework maps directly to the [Attack Trigger](../concepts/attack-trigger.md):
- Ball above net → can use flat (lowest margin, only from dominant position)
- Ball at net → use topspin for safety (higher margin than flat)
- Ball below net → never use flat; use topspin or slice to generate lift and arc

The [Topspin-Flat-Slice Framework](../concepts/topspin-flat-slice-framework.md) exists to provide the highest-margin shot for each situation. Flat is not wrong — it just has no margin for error, so using it below the net or from non-dominant positions multiplies errors.

## Common High-Error Habits Tyler Identifies

From the stubborn habits and "things that ruin your game" videos:
- **Trying to win the 3rd shot drive outright** — the drive is an approach shot, not a winner
- **Speeding up on the wrong ball** — forcing aggression below the net or on jammed contact
- **Aiming at lines instead of zones** — reduces margin for no benefit
- **Not committing to the drop** — partial commitment produces the worst outcome (a pop-up)
- **Giving up after a pop-up** — "the point is not over"

## The Win-Without-Hitting-Harder Mindset

From the Apr 30, 2026 video: most players lose by forcing winners instead of creating opportunities through smart placement. The fundamental insight: **control the rally construction, and points become easy**. Forcing winners from non-ideal positions is where the errors come from.
