# Footwork System

Tyler's core thesis: **"Every problem in pickleball is a footwork problem."** Popping up? Reaching — feet didn't move behind the ball. Can't get to the kitchen? Flat-footed transition. Getting jammed? Not setting up before the ball arrives.

The footwork system applies everywhere — kitchen, transition zone, baseline, return of serve.

## Kitchen Footwork

**Weight on toes, leaning forward.** Not heels. Chest forward, butt back. This lowers the center of gravity, allows taking balls earlier and more in front, and enables a forward swing rather than an upward swing (which pops balls up).

**Three options at the kitchen for wide balls:**
1. **One small side step** — for balls slightly off-center
2. **Cross step** — for wider balls, cross the far foot behind the near foot to cover distance
3. **Full shuffle across** — for balls well outside reach, full crab-walk movement

Always get the body behind the ball before making contact out in front. Reaching (arm out while body stays square) is the most common cause of pop-ups at the kitchen.

**The "cliff behind you" rule (from dink mechanics).** Never turn your back at the kitchen. Lateral movement only — crab walk side to side. Turning to chase a wide ball concedes the kitchen and puts you out of position.

**Paddle as measuring stick** (from the volleys/feet video): extend the paddle straight out. If the ball is in front of the paddle tip → take it off the bounce. If the ball is at or behind the tip → take it out of the air as a volley. This real-time tool removes hesitation from the volley/bounce decision.

## Baseline-to-Kitchen Transition

**Advance in waves, not a sprint.** This is the most misunderstood footwork principle for intermediate players.

The correct sequence: hit the shot → watch the opponent → when they hit, **stop** (split stop / split step) → assess where the ball is going → advance again when you know direction → repeat.

Running straight through without stopping leads to being caught mid-transition with the ball at your feet in the "no man's land" zone.

**Split stop / split step timing:** land on two feet simultaneously as the opponent is making contact. This freezes your momentum so you can change direction to follow the ball. Landing too early (before they hit) = frozen in place. Landing too late = already moving the wrong way.

## Return of Serve Footwork

Many players get jammed on returns because they start too close to the ball's landing zone or wait for the ball to come to them.

**The fix:** Turn the body, take a jab step toward the ball's path, then swing. Don't wait — go to the ball. The "catch drill" from the return of serve video: put the paddle down, have a partner serve, and physically move forward to catch it with two hands. This forces the natural movement pattern.

**Stand further back than you think, then move forward.** For topspin serves (ball kicks high) — moving forward intercepts the ball before the kick takes full effect. For slice serves (ball dies and veers) — moving forward cuts off the movement before it drifts. For power serves — starting back gives time to react.

## Drive and 3rd Shot Footwork

**Paddle back FIRST, then move.** The order matters. Take the paddle back (turn sideways) → THEN move to the ball. Reversing this order (run to the ball, then try to take the paddle back) = getting jammed every time. "Put your car in drive before you hit the gas."

The paddle take-back is the non-negotiable first step. This applies to both drives and drops.

## Adjustment Steps

**Constant, small adjustments right up until contact.** Especially in wind and on unpredictable bounces. Don't pre-plant and wait. Keep moving small steps until the moment of impact. "Adjustment steps are constant."

Players who plant early and wait for the ball commit before they know exactly where it will arrive, forcing compensations mid-swing.

## The "Imaginary Extended Kitchen Line"

For transition zone balls (approaching the kitchen, not yet at it): imagine a line 1–2 feet inside the baseline. Anything in front of that line → take off the bounce. Anything at or behind it → take out of the air. This prevents the chronic mistake of letting balls bounce at your feet when a volley would have been the better choice.

## The Root Cause Diagnostic

| Symptom | Footwork cause |
|---|---|
| Popping up at the kitchen | Reaching — feet didn't move behind the ball |
| Balls at your feet | Not advancing in waves — caught mid-transition |
| Jammed on drives | Paddle back after moving, not before |
| Jammed on returns | Waiting for ball instead of moving to it |
| Weak volleys | Flat-footed, not on toes, no forward lean |
