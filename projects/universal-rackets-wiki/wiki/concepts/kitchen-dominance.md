# Kitchen Dominance

Tyler's most comprehensive topic area — 9+ videos across the year. Kitchen dominance is both a physical and strategic concept: the right body position makes the right shot possible; the right strategy creates the right body position.

## The Physical Foundation

**Weight on toes, leaning forward.** Not heels. Chest over the kitchen line, butt back. This enables:
- Taking balls earlier and more in front of the body
- A forward swing path rather than an upward swing (which pops balls up)
- Quick lateral pushes to cover wide balls
- "Hovering over the kitchen line" — the aggressive stance Tyler associates with dominance

**Paddle out in front and away** — not just "up." The common failure: paddle is up but close to the body, so you still have to reach out to the ball (making it a passive position, not an aggressive one). "Hugging a tree / airing out your armpits." Paddle must be extended far enough that the ball meets the paddle rather than the paddle chasing the ball.

**Elbow tucked.** Limits backswing, keeps the paddle compact, and generates wrist speed without the arm-swing arc that causes shots to sail wide.

## The Dink: Push, Not Hit

"The moment someone thinks they have to hit is the moment they take a giant backswing and pop it up." The dink is a **shoulder-driven push**, not a wrist flip or full arm swing.

**Mechanics:**
- Think *forward*, not *upward* — push ball across the net, don't lift it
- No backswing, no looping motion — paddle stays out front
- Open paddle face slightly to lift the ball over the net (only works with the forward push motion)
- Lateral body movement only — crab walk; never turn your back ("cliff behind you")
- Contact always out in front and away from the body

**The "skimming the net" visualization:** Imagine the ball skimming just over the net tape. This single cue forces the forward-push motion and reduces pop-ups dramatically.

**Dink height limit:** Never higher than a paddle's length above the net. Higher than this and the opponent has an attack window.

## The Attack Window

The goal of every dink rally: **create a ball the opponent must hit from below the net**, forcing them to pop it up. The pop-up is your green light. See [Attack Trigger](../concepts/attack-trigger.md).

**How to create it:**
- Side-to-side dinking moves opponents wide — eventually they reach, contact is compromised
- Hitting behind moving opponents (they move away from the ball, forced to hit off-balance)
- Aiming at the transition zone (the opponent's feet) forces a low-contact defensive response

**When you get the pop-up: commit.** Drop center of gravity, wrist snap down, elbow tucked. Aim at the opponent's body or feet, not the lines. "I would rather have you miss long going for it than tap the ball back." Tap-backs train opponents not to fear popping up.

## The "Paddle as Measuring Stick" Decision

From multiple kitchen videos: extend the paddle straight out in ready position. If the ball is in front of the paddle tip → take it off the bounce. If at or behind the paddle tip → take it out of the air as a volley. This removes hesitation in real-time.

Also: having the paddle extended (rather than at your side) **shrinks the kitchen visually** for your opponent — less open court to aim at.

## The Red Light / Green Light Rule at the Kitchen

From the May 2026 kitchen domination video — no yellow light:
- **Ball at or above the net: green light.** Attack immediately. Any hesitation loses the time advantage.
- **Ball below the net: red light.** Block or reset. Do not force aggression.

## The Speed-Up Decision

**Only speed up on slow, floaty balls.** Timing the speed-up:
- Move back slightly to create space and allow contact out in front
- Use the forehand groundstroke motion (turn, load, transfer weight) — not just a wrist flick
- Aim at the opponent's chest — causes jamming even if not perfectly placed
- Immediately move forward to be ready for the "cleanup" pop-up that results from a good speed-up

**The dink/speed-up disguise (from Kitchen Strategy Masterclass):** Make dinks look like speed-ups (step back, pause, then dink) and speed-ups look like dinks. "Either make your dinks look like your speedups, or your speedups look like your dinks." Holding the shot until the last moment eliminates opponent anticipation.

## After a Pop-Up: Pop-Up Defense Protocol

Even kitchen dominance does not guarantee perfect execution. When you pop a ball up:
- **3 steps back + low paddle.** Immediately.
- "Seat down, paddle down." Knees bent, paddle low. A smash is coming down — paddle needs to be low to redirect it back up.
- The block is not a counter-attack — absorb the pace and redirect the ball up to clear the net. This resets the rally.
- "The point is NOT over when you pop the ball up."
