# Offensive Roll Volley

The offensive roll volley solves the most confusing problem in kitchen play: how to be aggressive from a contact point that is *below* net height. Most players think below-net = defensive only. Tyler teaches that below-net can still be offensive — if you use the right mechanics.

## The Core Problem

When a ball is below the net at the kitchen, the player must swing upward to get it over — which looks like a defensive, floating shot. The instinct is to swing forward (drive it), which sends the ball into the net from that contact height. Or to swing upward gently, producing a slow, floaty return.

The roll volley solves this by using **topspin mechanics from a close range** to generate both the height needed to clear the net and the pace needed to put pressure on the opponent.

## Mechanics

**Paddle tip DOWN before contact.** This is the entry point. The paddle does not start high or extended in a ready position — it is intentionally angled downward so the face can brush under and up through the ball.

**Explode upward with the legs.** Drop center of gravity simultaneously with the paddle tip going down. The leg explosion is the power source — the arm and wrist only guide direction.

**Finish paddle tip UP.** Paddle goes from tip-down to tip-up through contact — same arc as the topspin drive-drop ("drip"), just from a shorter distance. The ball goes forward as a result of the spin and the upward swing, not as a result of swinging at the target.

**Not swinging forward; not swinging down.** The roll volley is the upward brush (6 to 12 o'clock motion) applied at close range. Tyler: "Being offensive from below the net requires getting under the ball and brushing upward."

**Wrist snap:** The wrist actively drives from paddle-face-down to paddle-face-up through contact. This wrist motion combined with the leg explosion creates the topspin that makes the ball arc up over the net and then dip down aggressively.

## When to Use vs. Other Shots

| Situation | Shot |
|---|---|
| Ball well above net height | Put-away volley (wrist snap down, flat) |
| Ball at net height, space available | Speed-up (flat, contact out front) |
| Ball slightly below net, time available | Roll volley (tip down, upward brush) |
| Ball at feet, no space | Block/reset (square face, absorb) |

The roll volley sits between the speed-up (flat, dominant) and the block (defensive). It is the offensive choice when you have the ball slightly below the net but enough space and time to execute the upward brush.

## The Two Triggers for Being Aggressive

From the Offensive Masterclass (Feb 2026):
1. Ball above net height — the obvious trigger
2. Ball **away from your body** — even if below the net, space = aggressive potential

The roll volley applies in trigger #2 situations. If the ball is jammed close to the body, even the roll volley is not possible — can only block. Create space by stepping back if jammed. See [Attack Trigger](../concepts/attack-trigger.md).

## Relation to the Hybrid Drive-Drop

The roll volley is a compact, short-range version of the drip (hybrid drive-drop). The swing path is identical (6 to 12); the distance from the net is shorter; the contact point is closer. Both use leg explosion as the power source.

Players who learn the drip from the baseline first often find the roll volley at the kitchen easier to learn afterwards — the muscle memory for the tip-down/tip-up path is already established.

## Common Mistakes

**Swinging forward instead of upward.** This sends the ball into the net. The path must be genuinely upward — not forward at a slight upward angle.

**Standing tall.** Without leg bend, there is nothing to explode from. Standing tall + wrist alone = impossible. Leg explosion is non-negotiable.

**Using it in red-light situations.** The roll volley requires time and space. A hard, fast ball coming at your body cannot be rolled — only blocked. Players who try to roll volley every ball will pop up on anything fast.
