# Reset and Defense

Tyler's reset philosophy: **absorb and redirect**. The goal of a reset is not to counter-attack — it is to neutralize the rally and get back to a dinking or neutral state. Resets are a specific technique, not a reactive panic shot.

## Core Mechanics

**Paddle out in front and away from the body.** "You can't reset a ball if you're hitting it — the goal of the reset is to hold your paddle there." The paddle meets the ball; the ball does not chase the paddle.

**No backswing — ever.** "There is no time in pickleball for a backswing when someone is attacking." Elbow tucked to eliminate the backswing reflex. Players fail resets primarily because they instinctively take the paddle back when frightened by an incoming hard shot.

**Square (neutral) paddle face** for resets — NOT open like for dinks. An open face on a reset creates a pop-up. The neutral/square face redirects the ball low and cross-court.

**Don't flinch.** Get into an athletic low stance to absorb pace rather than getting pushed backward by the ball. Bending at the knees (not the waist) drops the center of gravity and allows swinging forward rather than upward.

## The Three Common Failure Modes

From the Apr 2026 reset video — all three have one cause (not being in proper position):

1. **Too big a backswing.** Usually happens when the reset is a reactive shot and the old drive/dink habit kicks in.
2. **Pulling the paddle away at contact.** Flinching — the ball arrives fast and the player recoils instead of holding position.
3. **Wrong paddle face angle.** Often too open, causing a pop-up. Square face for resets; open face for dinks.

## The "Follow Your Shot" Principle

From the Apr 2026 reset video — Tyler calls this "a huge change in my game":

After hitting any shot, point the paddle toward where the ball went. This naturally pre-positions the paddle for the incoming reset shot. If you dink crosscourt, your paddle finishes tracking crosscourt — already halfway to the reset position for the return dink.

"Follow the ball with your paddle after you hit." This eliminates the gap between shots where players drop the paddle or bring it back to center before repositioning.

## Handling Balls at Your Feet

From the Mar 2026 feet video:

- **Bend at the KNEES, not the waist.** Bending at the waist + swinging down forces an upward swing path = pop-up. Bending at the knees + swinging forward = ball stays lower.
- **Paddle as measuring stick.** Extend paddle: if ball is in front → take off the bounce. If at or behind → take out of the air. Removes the hesitation that gets players caught.
- **Use the imaginary extended kitchen line.** Commit to either taking it out of the air or letting it bounce — no half-commitments.

## Blocking Smashes (Pop-Up Defense)

When you've popped a ball up and a smash is incoming:

- 3 steps back immediately
- "Seat down, paddle down" — knees bent, paddle LOW (not up)
- Open face, absorb the pace, redirect upward to clear the net
- The lower the incoming ball is aimed, the lower the paddle must be

The block is a soft redirect, not a counter-hit. See [Kitchen Dominance](../concepts/kitchen-dominance.md) for the full pop-up defense protocol.

## Resetting Against Bangers

From the Mar 2026 volley video: when a ball comes hard and deep (no time to step back):
- Squeeze grip slightly
- Open paddle face so the laser points up
- Simply redirect it — no swing, no overthinking
- Block and redirect: don't try to speed up or over-swing

**"Redirect bangers with a low return — forces them to hit up, stopping the attack."**

The goal against a banger is not to out-bangle them (that feeds into their strength). Reset low, make them hit upward, neutralize the exchange. See [Attack Trigger](../concepts/attack-trigger.md) for when to shift from defense back to offense.

## Progressive Drill

From the Apr 2026 reset video: start at 20% pace with a partner, build to 100%. The order of operations at each pace level: get the position right, then add pace. Never try to reset at full pace without the mechanics locked in at lower pace.
