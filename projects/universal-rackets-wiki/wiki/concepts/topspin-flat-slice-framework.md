# Topspin-Flat-Slice Framework

Tyler's foundational spin system. The central premise: every spin has one job. Using the wrong spin for the situation is the most common mistake club players make. The golf club analogy — having only one spin type is like playing with one club.

## The Three Spins

### Topspin
**Swing path:** 6 o'clock to 12 o'clock. Paddle face slightly closed (tilted down toward ball). Contact the back of the ball with an upward brushing motion.

**When to use:** Serves, returns, drives, 3rd shot drops (topspin variant). Virtually any ball where you have time.

**Why:** Acts as an **insurance policy**. Same swing speed as flat but the ball dips into the court. Ball bounces near the baseline and kicks high, forcing the returner back. Off a volley, it stays low and difficult. Disrupts the opponent's contact point in both directions.

**The counterintuitive part:** Swing *twice as fast* with topspin compared to what feels right. Because you're only getting a fraction of the ball (brushing, not hitting through it), you need faster paddle speed to generate pace. Going slow removes the spin and the ball floats.

**Common mistake:** Swinging the same speed as flat — ball loops uselessly over the net or into the net.

### Flat
**Swing path:** Straight forward. Full contact with the ball.

**When to use:** Speed-ups where you want to strip time from the opponent. Put-away volleys from fully dominant positions (ball well above net, contact out in front, no risk of error).

**Why:** Travels fastest. Zero margin for error — no spin to bring it down, no safety net.

**The warning:** A "flat" ball still has some spin by physics — the label means spin is insufficient to meaningfully affect trajectory. Reserve flat only for when you are already winning the point, not to try to win it.

**Common mistake:** Using flat for drives or serves. A 100% flat drive looks good but fails 80% of the time.

### Slice
**Swing path:** 12 o'clock to 6 o'clock. Paddle face open (tilted up). Come DOWN on the back of the ball.

**When to use:** 3rd shot drops (slice variant), returns of serve, change-of-pace serves. Any situation where you want to disrupt opponent rhythm or keep the ball low after the bounce.

**Why:** Backspin causes the ball to stay low and move away from the opponent after the bounce. Creates a "dying" ball quality. Forces opponents to hit up, making their attack harder.

**Slice drop is Tyler's preferred drop variant.** "By getting in the proper grip to get backspin, you're automatically going to open your paddle face — which forces me to lift the ball." The open face creates margin for height errors.

**The warning:** Never use slice when you want pace. Backspin floats the ball and gives the opponent more time. Slice is for disruption and control, not power.

**Ball direction with slice:** The ball curves in the direction *opposite* your swing direction. Swing left → ball curves right.

## Decision Framework

| Situation | Use |
|---|---|
| Serve | Topspin (default) or slice (change-of-pace) |
| Return | Topspin (drive return) or slice (drop return) |
| 3rd shot drive | Topspin at 60–70% |
| 3rd shot drop | Slice (preferred) or topspin |
| Speed-up | Flat |
| Put-away volley (dominant position) | Flat |
| Below-net roll volley | Topspin (drip motion) |
