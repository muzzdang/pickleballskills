# 3 Things Pickleball Players Do That Ruin Their Game (Universal Rackets)

**Source:** YouTube
**Channel:** [Universal Rackets](../entities/universal-rackets.md)

**Uploaded:** 2026-02-15

## Key Takeaways

Titled "3 Things" but contains a comprehensive dinking breakdown. The three primary problems:

1. **Unnecessary swing / too much movement.** Dinking is a push from A to B — 6 inches of movement max. Any additional backswing, wrist, or shoulder motion adds uncontrollable variables. Fix: do as little as possible. Think "push," not "hit."
2. **Wrong paddle face.** Using the same closed/drive grip at the kitchen. When making contact below the net (most dinks), you need the paddle face open. "Arrow out the center of your paddle — when low, arrow must point up." Opposite for high balls: close the face.
3. **Not moving feet (or moving feet wrong).** Beginners learned "take as little steps as possible" — correct at first to prevent falling into the kitchen. But at intermediate level, that turns into reaching. Reaching = popping up. Fix: get your BODY behind the ball, THEN contact out in front and away.

- **Dink = "push," think "compact, concise, controlled."** Less is more.
- **Recover to the center after every dink.** Tyler's own mistake in 2023: drilling crosscourt only, then getting speeded up down the middle.
- **Three targets when dinking:** (1) out wide to pull opponent off court; (2) middle to create miscommunication; (3) directly at opponent's body/feet — underused but highly effective.
- **Get low to swing forward.** Waist-bend → must swing up → pops up. Leg-bend → can swing forward → stays low.
- **Limit the wrist entirely.** Push with arm and shoulder (more specifically, the chest and shoulder).

## Visual Notes

Tyler demonstrates recovery to center after each crosscourt dink. He shows the difference between waist-bend (pop-up prone) and leg-bend (forward swing enabled). The three targets shown on court with specific ball placement for each.
