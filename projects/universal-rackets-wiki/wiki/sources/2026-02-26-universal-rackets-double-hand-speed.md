# Double Your Hand Speed in Pickleball (Universal Rackets)

**Source:** YouTube
**Channel:** [Universal Rackets](../entities/universal-rackets.md)
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hwhulIeFdjw
**Duration:** 9:20
**Uploaded:** 2026-02-26

## Key Takeaways

Hand speed is largely a positioning problem, not a reflexes problem:

- **Tip 1: Start and finish in the same position.** The main reason players feel slow: after they hit a ball, their paddle ends up somewhere different from where it started. Then the next ball comes and they have to move the paddle to position again. "Wherever I start, I finish. Wherever I finish, I start."
- **Tip 2: Use the wrist, not the arm and shoulder.** Dinking is shoulder-driven. Volleys are wrist-driven. Players learn shoulder motion for dinking and carry it to volleys, where they need rapid wrist snaps. Tuck elbow into body, hold paddle out, use ONLY the wrist.
- **Tip 3: Tuck the elbow.** "The moment my elbow goes outside my body is the moment I take an unnecessary big swing." Drill: put a pickleball under your armpit and try to hold it there while hitting volleys.
- **Tip 4: Lean forward / weight on toes.** Leaning forward = contacting the ball earlier and farther out in front. Being passive/leaning back = ball gets close, jammed, late.
- **Tip 5: Know what is a forehand and what is a backhand.** Many players take unnecessary forehands from close to the body. "80% of your shots in pickleball are a backhand." Anything close to the body or across = backhand.
- **Tip 6 (implied): 80% backhand.** Train yourself to default to backhand — it's the faster, more natural response to most incoming balls at the kitchen.

## Visual Notes

Tyler demonstrates the elbow tuck with armpit pickleball drill. Shows the forehand vs. backhand range-of-motion comparison — everything toward the middle and body side of center = backhand. Demonstrates weight-on-toes vs. heels: lean forward and contact point is 2 feet further forward.
