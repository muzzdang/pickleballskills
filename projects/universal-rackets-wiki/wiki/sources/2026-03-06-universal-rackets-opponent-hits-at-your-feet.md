# What To Do When Your Opponent Hits at Your Feet (Universal Rackets)

**Source:** YouTube
**Channel:** [Universal Rackets](../entities/universal-rackets.md)
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-6Ds4zLboY0
**Duration:** 22:23
**Uploaded:** 2026-03-06

## Key Takeaways

- **Hit forward instead of up.** The core fix: whatever goes down must go up. Bending at the waist + swinging down forces you to swing upward = pop-up. Instead, bend at the KNEES + swing FORWARD = ball stays lower. Even a small leg bend is "night and day."
- **Paddle-as-measuring-stick for volley vs. bounce decision.** Paddle straight out in front: if the ball is in front of the paddle tip → take off the bounce (dink). If at or behind the paddle tip → take it out of the air (volley). This real-time tool, used constantly by pros, eliminates hesitation.
- **The imaginary extended kitchen line.** For balls that will bounce, place an imaginary line 1-2 feet inside the baseline. Anything in front of that line → take off the bounce. Anything at or behind that line → take out of the air. Prevents the chronic mistake of letting balls bounce at your feet when you should have taken them out of the air.
- **Open paddle face for low bouncing balls.** Same principle as dinking: low contact requires open face to lift ball over net. Squeeze grip slightly to maintain paddle stability.
- **Get the body behind the ball.** Not just the paddle in front — get your body positioned behind the contact point. Then contact out in front and away from the body.
- **Use the transition zone wisely.** This video is largely about handling balls in the transition zone — where the ball bounces at your feet because you're between the baseline and kitchen.

## Visual Notes

Tyler uses poly spots to mark the imaginary extended kitchen line. He sets up the paddle-as-measuring-stick position — arm extended, paddle out. Shows opponent at kitchen dropping a ball toward his feet — demonstrates the bend-at-waist (pop-up result) vs. bend-at-knees (forward shot) contrast.
