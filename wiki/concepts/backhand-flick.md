# Backhand Flick

## Definition

An aggressive, wrist-driven backhand speed-up hit from at or behind the kitchen line. The ball is struck with a low-to-high "windshield wiper" motion that generates topspin while lifting the ball just over the net. Distinguished from the [roll shot](roll-shot.md) by its use of wrist rotation rather than shoulder drive.

## How to Hit It

### Grip
Continental or between continental and eastern. [Mari Humberg](../entities/mari-humberg.md) recommends gripping at whatever position you naturally arrive at the kitchen — she has recently started choking up but notes it is not required.

### The Motion
1. Open stance with shoulder turned (unit turn) — you cannot hit a backhand flick with an open chest
2. Drop the paddle to contact the ball well out in front of the body with elbow extended (not bent)
3. Drive the paddle from low to high in a windshield-wiper arc — this generates topspin and sufficient clearance
4. Do not push forward; the rotation upward is the power source, not forward arm extension

### Teaching Drill
Stand at the net, place the paddle face against the net, and flick upward. No forward force — only upward rotation. This trains the correct wrist path before adding any pace.

### When to Use It
- Ball is between **knee and hip height** — ideal window
- Ball below the knee: do not attempt; the low-to-high path will struggle to clear the net and control is poor
- Opponent's dink sits up slightly — the flick punishes it with pace and spin

## Four Target Zones (from left side of court)

1. **Down the line** → opponent's right hip (very uncomfortable to handle)
2. **Down the middle** → facing player's left shoulder or crosscourt opponent's right shoulder
3. **Full crosscourt** → at the opponent's body
4. **Short angle crosscourt** → pushes opponent completely off court; hardest to execute but clean winner if correct

## Flick vs. Roll

| | Backhand Flick | Roll Shot |
|---|---|---|
| Power source | Wrist rotation | Shoulder drive |
| Feel | Snappy, compact | Smooth, shoulder-led |
| When used | Ball between knee-hip, needs surprise | Ball at hip-chest, more setup time |
| Primary teacher | Mari Humberg | Multiple coaches |

## Common Mistakes

- Hitting the flick when ball is below the knee (go for a reset instead)
- Closed stance — must turn shoulder to generate wrist power
- Contact too close to the body — lose wrist leverage
- Combining flick and roll mechanics randomly (see [Cori Elliott's breakdown](../sources/2026-05-22-cori-elliott-backhand-flick-vs-roll.md))
