# Backhand

The backhand is statistically the weaker side for most recreational pickleball players. Universal Rackets has produced the most thorough backhand content in the wiki, organized as a system of checkpoints and scenario-specific applications.

## The 5 Backhand Checkpoints

Universal Rackets' systematic framework for diagnosing and fixing backhand problems:

1. **Grip** — Continental or Eastern backhand grip closes the paddle face naturally at contact
2. **Stance / Unit Turn** — body must be sideways; shoulders turned; never open/square for a drive
3. **Take-back** — compact, not a full tennis backswing; paddle head up, wrist firm
4. **Contact** — out in front of the lead hip, not beside the body
5. **Follow-through** — paddle face direction determines ball direction; finish toward target

See: [5 Backhand Checkpoints (Universal Rackets, 2026-02-06)](../sources/2026-02-06-universal-rackets-5-backhand-checkpoints.md)

## Backhand Drive

The backhand drive requires active shoulder rotation — unlike the forehand, the backhand does not load naturally into hip rotation. Key mechanics:
- Unit turn: both shoulders must rotate to load power
- Contact out in front (consistent with all groundstrokes)
- Don't arm the drive — if the shoulders don't rotate, there's no power and the ball tends to go short

The backhand drive is covered in both the reliable 3rd shot drive video and the complete backhand masterclass.

## Backhand Dink

Uses the same shoulder-driven push as the forehand dink — but the backhand dink keeps the paddle face square more naturally. Key checkpoints:
- Elbow in front of body, not tucked behind
- Open face slightly to get ball up and over the net
- Push forward, not upward

## Backhand Volley

The weakest backhand scenario for most players. Key mechanics:
- Compact motion — no backswing at the kitchen
- Elbow tucks, face angle is critical
- Less wrist action than forehand volley; rely on paddle face direction
- See [Volley](volley.md) for the full fast-hands system

## Common Errors

- **Arming the drive:** no shoulder rotation, weak contact, ball goes short or sails
- **Contact beside the body:** racket arm fully extended sideways → no leverage → inconsistent direction
- **Open stance for the drive:** facing forward on a drive removes all body rotation potential
- **Inconsistency under pressure:** the backhand breaks down in fast exchanges — the cause is almost always contact point wandering (beside body instead of in front)
