# Bump Dink

## What It Is

The bump is an alternative to the slice when taking a ball out of the air at the kitchen line. Instead of chopping down on the ball to create backspin (slice), you push *forward* through it using the shoulder — keeping the paddle face open or neutral and driving through with a locked wrist.

Emerged as a meaningful weapon in the last 6-12 months (as of 2026), driven by new paddle tech that makes rollers more dangerous. Richard Pickleball went from using it 0% to 50%+ of the time at the NVZ in roughly 12 months.

## Why Use It Instead of the Slice

| Situation | Slice | Bump |
|---|---|---|
| Opponent is a roller (2H backhand) | Ball is dead, they back up and roll off it | Ball arrives faster, disrupts their setup |
| Opponent reads your slice early | They set up and roll aggressively | No time to set up — they're forced into a short hop |
| Need more reach forward | Wrist cocked back limits extension | Paddle forward = more reach |
| Opponent crashing the net | Easy to attack a dead slice | Harder to flick/attack a bump |
| You're out of position / moving | — | Slice preferred — bump requires being set |

## Technique

### Setup
1. **Point the paddle forward** — not angled down (that's a slice). Face roughly perpendicular to the target direction.
2. **Loosen wrist slightly** — not limp, just relaxed. Then lock it completely once the shot begins.

### Pre-shot face adjustment (hinge only)
Adjust face angle based on ball height *before* initiating the shot:
- **Higher ball** → slightly more closed face (paddle tilts down)
- **Lower ball** → more open face (paddle tilts up)

This hinge happens before the shoulder starts moving — not during.

### Execution
3. **All shoulder drive** — from setup, push through the ball using only the shoulder. No wrist snap, no roll, no flick. The shoulder is the engine.
4. **Lock the wrist completely** once the forward motion begins.

## Target Zone

**Between the middle of the NVZ box and the middle of the opponent's feet** — forward and central.

**Avoid going wide:** Going wide with the bump requires closing the face and becomes more like a roll — harder, less reliable, more likely to miss. Keep bumps forward and central.

## Against Rollers (Primary Use Case)

The bump is most effective against players who roll the two-handed backhand cross-court. Against them:
1. They read a slice → back up early → roll aggressively off the dead ball
2. Against the bump → ball arrives with pace → they're forced to short-hop it → you regain control

The bump at the feet also makes it harder for them to attack: they can only flick it at your body (not cross-court), so even if they attack you can slide and counter.

## Reach Advantage

The slice requires the wrist cocked back, limiting forward extension. The bump, with the paddle pointed forward, allows significantly more reach. Use this to push the ball toward the feet of a player who isn't fully established at the NVZ — it's hard to slice that direction but easy to bump.

## When to Still Slice

- When you're **on the move**, out of position, or uncomfortable
- When you're **stretched out wide** — the slice is a better neutralizer when you're off-balance
- The bump requires you to be **set** and expecting the ball
