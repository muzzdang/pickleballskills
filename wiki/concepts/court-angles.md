# Court Angles

Shots hit on a sharp diagonal that pull opponents wide off the court — often unreachable if timed correctly. The geometry of the court means a tight angle from near the sideline can land in bounds while landing completely outside an opponent's reach.

## The critical rule: earn the angle

Angles only work **after** you've established control with center balls. Going for a tight angle too early — before moving the opponent or setting up the point — leads to errors because there's no margin for mistake and the opponent is still in position.

The sequence: center → move opponent → center → angle when they're off-balance.

## Where angles are most effective

- When an opponent is reluctant to approach the kitchen (staying back at the baseline) — a tight angle to the sideline forces a very long run
- After a [side-to-side dinking](side-to-side-dinking.md) sequence pushes them wide — double down on that side with a sharp angle

## Kitchen vs transition

At the kitchen, angles are mostly lateral. In the [transition zone](transition-zone.md), there's a full rectangle to work with — both width and depth — making angles more varied and harder to cover.
