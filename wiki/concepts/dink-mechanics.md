# Dink Mechanics

A dink is a **push**, not a hit. The single most common mistake at beginner/intermediate level is treating the dink like a groundstroke — big swing, wrist involvement, full arm motion. All of that leads to pop-ups.

## The push model

Think underarm toss, bean bag throw, bowling motion. The stroke is shoulder-driven, not wrist or arm:
- Shoulder initiates, arm follows
- Paddle goes from **here to here** — minimal travel
- Think **forward**, not upward — push the ball forward across the net, don't lift it

## Paddle face

Angle the paddle face slightly **open** (tilted back) so the ball naturally rides up and over the net. Combined with a forward push (not a lift), this is the complete stroke. If you skip straight to open paddle face without the forward push, you'll pop every ball.

## Body position

- **Lateral movement only** — crab walk side to side; never turn your back to the net, never step backward ("cliff behind you" mental model)
- **Paddle stays out in front at all times** — no backswing, no looping motion; if you take the paddle back or loop on the backhand, contact happens beside or behind the body instead of out front
- **Lean forward** — weight toward the net, not back on heels
- For the backhand: the paddle stays out front and the upper body rotates to shift it, not a pulling-back motion

## Point of contact

Regardless of how elaborate the pre-contact setup looks (e.g., Ben Johns turning sideways before his backhand dink), the ball must always be contacted **out in front of the body**. Contact beside or behind the body = pop-up or net.

## Drills

1. **Toss drill** — physically toss the ball over the net with your hand; check that fingertips point forward at finish (not up or sideways). Then replicate with paddle.
2. **No-backswing drill** — keep paddle out front at all times, even if it feels awkward. Sustain 20-40 ball rallies this way.
3. **Sustained dinking** — paddle barely moves, face open, push forward; build consistency before worrying about placement.

## Taking Balls Out of the Air: Slice vs. Bump

When a dinkable ball reaches you at the NVZ before it bounces, you have two options:

**Slice (traditional):** High-to-low chopping path, backspin, decelerating ball. Good when you're on the move or off-balance. Allows opponents who read it to back up and roll aggressively off the dead ball.

**Bump (emerging):** Paddle pointed forward, shoulder push, little to no spin, more pace. Better when you're set and expecting a roll. Takes away the opponent's time, denies the step-back-and-roll read, and gives you more forward reach.

The bump has become increasingly common at higher levels (2025-2026) as two-handed backhand rolling has become dominant. See: [Bump Dink](bump-dink.md)
