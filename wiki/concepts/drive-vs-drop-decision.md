# Drive vs. Drop Decision (3rd Shot)

The most important decision in pickleball on every third shot. The wrong choice — even with perfect execution — loses the point structurally.

## The Master Rule

> **Opponent deep / not established → drive.**
> **Opponent up and established → drop.**

Most players ignore the other side of the net and decide based only on the ball. Liam Duffin's core teaching: always look at where your opponents are *first*, then decide.

## Decision by Return Depth

### Deep return (you're behind the baseline)
**→ Drive**

The kitchen looks tiny from back there. Don't pressure yourself to hit a reliable drop from distance. A drive:
- Gets the ball on your opponent while they're still moving forward
- Produces an easier volley reply (they're off-balance)
- Gets you to the kitchen in 1-2 shots vs. 3+ shots on a drop attempt from deep

### Short return (mid-court, inside the baseline)
**→ Depends on opponent position**

| Opponent position | Shot |
|---|---|
| Back / not established | Drive (keep them back) |
| Up and established at kitchen | Drop (driving = death sentence) |

**One-foot trigger:** If you have at least one foot inside the baseline and can lean forward through contact → you can drop.

## Decision by Height (Short Returns)

| Ball height | Best choice |
|---|---|
| Short + high (above net tape) | Drive — you can hit down on them even if they're at the kitchen |
| Short + low (at or below net tape) | Drop — driving means hitting up → they smash down |
| Short + medium | "Driving drop" at 40-50% pace (advanced, 5.0+ skill) |

## Decision by Spin Type

### Slice return → lean toward driving
- Ball stays low; bend knees, paddle tip toward ground to get beneath it
- **Spin continuation:** slice → your drive creates topspin → ball dips harder at opponent → harder to block
- Aim higher than normal (shoulder level) — the dip will bring it to their feet
- Dropping a slice requires near-perfect mechanics; popping it up under pressure is common
- Can still drop at 5.5+ level, but 1-in-20 at most levels

### Topspin return → lean toward dropping
- **Spin continuation:** topspin → your drop gets underspin → pillowy, consistent arc
- Much more reliable than dropping a slice
- "Way more consistent with a topspin return"
- If one foot inside baseline + forward lean through contact → drop it
- Can still drive if opponent is deep or return pace is extreme

## Pace Adjustment

| Position | Drive pace |
|---|---|
| Behind baseline (deep return) | 70% |
| Mid-court / transition zone | 40-50% |

From mid-court: lower pace = more control, ball still dips at feet. You're not generating pace — you're guiding a ball that already has spin.

## Opponent Preference Test

In the first 2-3 points of every match: test whether opponents prefer being driven at or dropped at. Most players hate one more than the other. Find it, exploit it.

> *"So many players don't look at the other side of the net. They're so focused on themselves."* — Liam Duffin

## Concede the Kitchen When Necessary

If the opponent is up and established and you don't have a clear drive: **concede**. Hit a dink, let them come in, and play from neutral. Don't force a 50/50 ball. Create offense from the kitchen exchange instead.
