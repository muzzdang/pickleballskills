# Floor and Ceiling

## The Model

Every player has two levels:
- **Ceiling** — your best possible play. When everything is clicking, shots are working, confidence is high.
- **Floor** — your worst play. In big tournaments, tight matches, bad conditions, off days.

The gap between them is what makes you inconsistent.

**The goal is not to raise the ceiling — it's to raise the floor.** Great players aren't defined by their ceiling (many players can play great pickleball occasionally). They're defined by how good they are on their worst day.

> *"Ben Johns is the GOAT because his worst day is still a good day — because he plays simple when he needs to."* — Richard Pickleball

## How to Raise Your Floor

The answer is **simplicity** — removing variables when you're tight, in a big match, or not feeling your best. Two areas:

### 1. Transition: Mush Drive → Push Drop

When your regular drop isn't landing (no feel, windy, tight), default to the **drive-drop sequence**:

**Mush drive:**
- No swing. Paddle starts in front of the body and pushes forward.
- Aim at opponent's chest/body — not one inch over the net.
- Zero variables = near-100% make rate.
- Easier to time up on fast returns than a full swing drive.

**Push drop (follow-up):**
- After they volley the drive (which will usually come back short/easy), open the paddle face.
- Lift at a 45-degree angle, no wrist, follow through forward not up.
- Walk directly into the kitchen.
- Works as a backhand too — same mechanics, same open face lift.

See: [Third Shot Drop](third-shot-drop.md), [Third Shot Drive](third-shot-drive.md)

### 2. Net Game: Push, Move, Reach

When feeling tight at the kitchen:

- **Replace rolls with push/slice dinks.** Topspin rolls are higher risk. Pushes and slices are reliable and still hard to attack.
- **Move the ball around.** Alternate spots (cross-court, side to side) to force foot movement. Players who struggle to move their feet will pop up.
- **Find offense out of the air.** Reach forward to intercept high balls as volleys. *Never pull off the bounce* when tight — "at 99 and the third, I'm never pulling off the bounce." Pulling off the bounce under pressure produces errors consistently.
- **Allow opponents to beat themselves.** If a ball is 50/50, dink one more time — a patient opponent may give you a free popup. You don't need to force every opportunity.

## Key Principle

> *"It's not about how good you are on your best day. It's about how good you are on your worst day."*

Raising the floor and raising the ceiling are not opposed — simplicity on bad days doesn't limit your ceiling on good days. It just prevents bad days from becoming catastrophic.
