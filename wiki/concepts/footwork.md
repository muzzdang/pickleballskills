# Footwork

Footwork is the most undercoached element in recreational pickleball and the biggest differentiator at intermediate levels. Good footwork makes all other technique easier; bad footwork forces compensations that create errors.

## Ready Position

**Athletic stance:** weight on the balls of the feet (not heels), knees slightly bent, paddle up and out front. This is the universal starting position at the kitchen — it allows movement in any direction.

**Never be flat-footed.** Heels on the ground = no reactive ability. The moment you settle back on your heels, you've slowed your first step by 20-30%.

## Split Step

The split step is the most important footwork skill in pickleball. See [Split Stop](split-stop.md) for the full breakdown.

**Key principle:** Split step BEFORE your opponent contacts the ball — not after you see where it's going. The split loads your legs for explosive movement in any direction.

## Kitchen Footwork: The Crab Walk

At the Non-Volley Zone line, movement is lateral — never turning your back to the net.

- **Crab walk:** sideways shuffle, staying square to the net, moving left or right to track the ball
- Stay sideways at all times — turning your back removes your ability to control the dink direction and opens your body to quick attacks
- Weight stays on the balls of the feet throughout lateral movement
- "Cliff behind you" mental cue: imagine a cliff directly behind your heels — you must not step back

## Transition Zone Footwork

Moving from the baseline to the kitchen requires controlled advance, not a sprint.

- Advance in split-step stops: move → split (as opponent contacts) → move → split
- Never sprint all the way to the kitchen in one motion — you'll be caught mid-stride when the opponent strikes
- The [transition zone](transition-zone.md) is the most dangerous position; controlled movement reduces time spent there
- After a third shot drop, advance with splits until reaching the NVZ line

## Feet at Contact

For drives and groundstrokes:
- Sideways body position at contact — never open/square for a drive
- Step into the ball with the inside foot to align the hip for natural swing direction
- For the down-the-line shot, step toward the target line; the body alignment creates the direction

## Feet-at-Body Defense

When the opponent hits at your feet:
- Bend the knees BEFORE the ball arrives — not as you're hitting it
- Players with straight legs have no mechanical way to get the paddle under the ball cleanly
- Bent knees allow a compact scoop upward that redirects the ball without a pop-up

## Box Step (Hands Battles)

In hands-battle situations at the kitchen, Austin Hardy (PPB) recommends the **box step** over crossing feet or lunging. Small steps in a box pattern keep the base wide and weight centered, allowing immediate directional change when the ball is redirected. See [Hands Battle](hands-battle.md).

## Tripod Foot (Kieran / Dinkcredibles)

Kieran introduces the **tripod foot** concept for dink mechanics: weight distributed across three points — heel, ball of foot under big toe, ball of foot under pinky toe. This activates the glutes and stabilizes the entire ankle-knee-hip chain. A collapsed arch (foot rolling inward) causes the knee to cave and destabilizes hip rotation — the root cause of many backhand dink yips.
