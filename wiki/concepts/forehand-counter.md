# Forehand Counter

## Definition

An aggressive forehand redirect used to counter a speed-up or drive directed at the forehand side during a firefight. One of the most technically challenging shots for players using a continental grip at the net.

## The Continental Grip Problem

Eastern and semi-western grips have a naturally tilted (closed) paddle face — easy to swing through on a forehand and control the ball's direction. **Continental grip** (recommended for net play) opens the paddle face on the forehand side. Most players compensate by trying to time a wrist snap at contact — this is unreliable, especially at pace.

The "slappy wrist" counter: wrist stays open → player tries to time a snap → ball flies out or goes wide. Very difficult to be consistent at speed.

## The Ping-Pong Model (Richard Pickleball)

Modeled after how a ping-pong player generates forehand power — with a **shoulder and arm unit turn**, not a wrist snap:

1. **Shoulder unit turn**: as the ball approaches, turn the shoulder slightly and bring the paddle back with the arm (not just the wrist)
2. **Wrist preset**: as the paddle comes back, close the wrist slightly *before the swing begins* — the wrist is set, not moving; the paddle face closes pre-contact, not during
3. **Arm acceleration**: drive through with the arm; the wrist stays locked and preset throughout
4. Cue: "First SET, then MOVE, then HIT" — never setting the wrist at the same time as moving

This feels stiff compared to the wrist-slap version, but is far more consistent across pace and height variations.

## Advanced: Paddle-Tip Drop for Height Variation

Once the basic preset is mastered, an additional refinement:
- Instead of preset with palm slightly facing down, close the wrist further so the paddle *tip points downward and outward* (as if pointing a finger down)
- This drops the paddle head slightly
- Result: the tip now matches ball height automatically — tip up for high balls, tip sideways for mid, tip down for low balls
- Enables the same swing path across all heights, which is otherwise impossible with an open-face continental grip
- Benefit on low balls: you can actually *attack* a low ball (instead of just blocking) because the tip and path work together

## Why This Works for Continental

On the backhand side in continental, being slightly late still produces a redirected ball (just goes crosscourt instead of down the line — manageable). On the forehand side in continental, being late sends the ball wide — there is no margin. The preset eliminates the timing sensitivity.

## Ben Johns Reference

[Richard Pickleball](../entities/richard-pickleball.md) cites [Ben Johns](../entities/ben-johns.md) as a player who executes this ping-pong forehand counter particularly well — compact, shoulder-driven, consistent across different heights and pace.

## Drill

Partner hits balls at varied heights (high/mid/low) — counter with the preset-wrist arm-drive motion. Focus on: (1) shoulder turn with the paddle, (2) preset wrist before the forward swing, (3) arm acceleration only. No wrist involvement during the swing itself.

## Common Mistakes

- Taking the paddle back with only the wrist (not turning the shoulder)
- Setting the wrist as you swing forward (not before)
- Open palm (not closing the face pre-contact) — ball flies out
- Hesitating at the last moment and switching back to a wrist slap under pressure
