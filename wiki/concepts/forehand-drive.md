# Forehand Drive

The forehand drive is the most natural shot for most players — and therefore the one where bad habits most stubbornly persist. Universal Rackets covers the forehand extensively, from rapid-fire tips to full masterclasses.

## Core Mechanics

**Low, hard, unreturnable — in that order.** All three properties come from mechanics, not effort:

- **Low trajectory:** achieved by contacting the ball below shoulder height with a level or slightly downward swing. [Topspin](topspin.md) helps bring the ball down after clearing the net.
- **Hard:** comes from the [kinetic chain](kinetic-chain.md) — weight transfer, hip rotation, shoulder rotation, arm, wrist snap at contact. Not from arm speed alone.
- **Unreturnable:** placement (wide, body, backhand corner) matters more than pace at most levels.

**Contact out in front.** In line with the front knee, not beside the body. Contact beside the body has no leverage and produces inconsistent direction. See [Contact Point](contact-point.md).

**Head down through contact.** Keep head down and forward at, during, and after contact. Lifting the head early pulls the body up and sends the ball long.

## Common Errors

- **Arm-only swing:** no body rotation → short, weak drives that sit up
- **Contact beside the body:** paddle arm fully extended sideways → no leverage
- **Hitting too hard:** full power creates inconsistency; 60-70% pace is the reliable drive
- **No follow-through:** stopping the swing at contact kills pace and direction

## Forehand Putaway Volley

The forehand's finishing shot at the kitchen — used when the ball is above net height and attackable. Compact swing, wrist snap at contact, aimed at a corner or the opponent's body.

See: [Forehand Putaway Volley (Universal Rackets, 2026-01-01)](../sources/2026-01-01-universal-rackets-forehand-putaway-volley.md)

## The Forehand as a Weapon

The forehand can be a consistent offensive weapon, not just a reactive stroke:
- Build a reliable crosscourt forehand first (highest percentage)
- Add the down-the-line forehand as a change-up once crosscourt is consistent
- Use angles: pull opponents wide, then close out the middle
