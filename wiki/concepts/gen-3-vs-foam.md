# Gen 3 vs Foam (Paddle Cores)

The two dominant paddle core technologies in 2026, each with distinct tradeoffs.

## Gen 3 (polypropylene / PP core)

The classic honeycomb polypropylene construction. Still widely used at the pro level.

**Feel:** Lively, connected, "poppy" — ball comes off with a crispness that foam doesn't replicate.
**Power:** Generally good power but can feel "springboard-like" — power isn't always linear.
**Weakness: Core crushing.** The PP honeycomb can compress and deform over time, losing performance. Some paddles core crush within months of heavy use.
**Who prefers it:** Players who like the feel of traditional paddles; those who dislike foam's damper feel.

## Foam core (EPP / EVA foam)

The newer construction, introduced around 2024-2025 by most major brands.

**Feel:** Softer, more dampened, more "dwell" (ball stays on face slightly longer). Some find it less lively.
**Power:** Often higher peak power; more predictable/linear power delivery.
**Durability:** Generally more durable than Gen 3; no core crushing issue.
**Variations:** EPP (expanded polypropylene foam), EVA foam, hybrid foam-in-foam designs (e.g., Honolulu's EP+EVA ring design, Six Zero's tectonic suspension).

## The debate

Gen 3 ≠ bad; foam ≠ good. They're different feels. Many pros continue to use Gen 3 (JOOLA Pro V, Ruby Pro, Franklin Aurelius). The best paddle is the one you're confident with.

JOOLA's decision to stay Gen 3 for the Pro V is a deliberate bet that the Gen 3 feel serves more players better — backed by their work reducing core crushing through carbon fiber reinforcement at the top of the paddle.

## Core crushing (Gen 3 specific)

Compression of the honeycomb cells that causes a "dead" feeling and loss of power/consistency. Signs: ball feels mushy, noticeably less pop. Occurs in the top portion of the paddle head where impact is concentrated. Addressed by: JOOLA adds carbon fiber reinforcement there; warranty coverage (JOOLA covers 3 replacements).

## Gen 3 Power Spectrum

Not all Gen 3 paddles are equal. There's a wide power spectrum within Gen 3:

- **Max power end (Gen 3):** Luzz Cannon — possibly the hardest-hitting Gen 3 paddle; sacrifices nearly all control; small sweet spot; mishits go dead. ~$99.
- **Balanced end (Gen 3):** JOOLA Pro V — intentionally softer than its predecessor (Pro IV); Gen 3 with carbon reinforcement to prevent core crush; control-oriented. ~$300.
- **Control/feel end (Gen 3):** Six Zero Ruby Pro — Gen 3 with tectonic suspension; soft, connected feel.
- **Control-first (Gen 4):** Beyond Measure Ronin — massive sweet spot, plush/marshmallow dwell, T700 carbon fiber face, enough pop to finish points but built around control. Short handle. See [Beyond Measure Ronin](../entities/beyond-measure-ronin.md).

The Luzz Cannon represents what happens at the extreme power end of Gen 3 thermoforming: maximum trampoline with minimal control compensation. Requires compact mechanics and lead tape to be usable for most players.
