# Grip

Grip position — where on the paddle handle the hand sits — is one of the most impactful and undercoached variables in pickleball. Unlike tennis, where grip style (eastern, western, continental) is the primary variable, pickleball requires dynamic grip position adjustments based on court location.

## Zone-Based Grip Position (Austin Hardy / PPB)

The key principle: **hang off the end at the baseline, choke up at the kitchen.**

| Zone | Grip Position | Why |
|------|--------------|-----|
| Baseline | Hang off the end of the handle | Maximum reach and power; slower exchanges allow full lever |
| Transition zone | Slide hand up mid-handle | Actively adjusting during approach |
| Kitchen line | Choked up (1–2 inches above normal) | Shorter lever = faster hand exchanges; more control |

This is a deliberate, zone-by-zone adjustment — not a fixed grip. The hand slides up the handle as the player approaches the kitchen, arriving at the choked position by the time they reach the NVZ line.

**Evidence from pro film:** Ben Johns and Gabe Tardio both demonstrably choke up at the kitchen. Austin Hardy cites Tardio as the elite example — his grip sits 1–2 inches above normal during hands battles.

## Hybrid Grip (Continental / Eastern)

The recommended base grip for pickleball is a **continental/eastern hybrid** — midway between a full continental (handle pointing up-down) and a full eastern (handle pointing toward you). This allows:
- Efficient transitions between forehand and backhand dinks without re-gripping
- A neutral paddle face that works for most shots without adjustment
- Natural wrist pronation for serve mechanics

Avoid full western grip — the extreme face angle requires re-gripping for most shots and creates inconsistency.

## Grip Pressure

- **At the kitchen:** Light grip pressure. Heavy pressure stiffens the wrist and kills dink touch. The "broken wrist" feel (Luke Larsen's cue for two-handed BH dinks) applies broadly.
- **At the baseline:** Slightly firmer for drive mechanics — wrist needs some firmness for control.
- **In hands battles:** Light grip pressure generally, firming only at contact for direction control.

## Choke Up = Faster Hands

The physics: choking up reduces the effective lever arm of the paddle. A shorter lever arm requires less force and time to move. This directly translates to faster hand exchanges at the kitchen. This is why pros consistently choke up in hands battles even if they don't consciously discuss it.

## Tungsten Tape Tip

Adding tungsten tape to the head of the paddle (not the handle) shifts the swing weight forward without changing handle feel. For players who need more power from a lighter paddle, this allows choking up (for kitchen speed) while maintaining baseline power. Reference: Pickleball Playbook grip video (2026-02-02).
