# Kitchen Aggression

Playing offensively at the kitchen requires a specific body position and mindset, not just skill. Most players can't be offensive because they're in the wrong position before the ball arrives.

## Body position: the foundation

**Weight on toes, not heels.** Lean forward over the kitchen line — hovering, not standing back. Paddle extended out in front. This makes you look intimidating and lets you take balls early before they drop.

**Get low in your legs, not at the waist.** Bending at the waist makes the ball pop up. Get into your legs and push *forward*, not down-and-up.

**Hold your ground.** Never turn your back to the net or step backward unless deliberately creating space for a speedup. The moment you retreat from the kitchen, you lose your offensive position.

## Taking the ball early

The more out in front you make contact, the more offensive the shot. Contact at your feet = defensive. Contact out in front = offensive.

**Paddle as a measuring stick:** at or in front of the paddle → let it bounce; at or behind the paddle → take it out of the air as a volley.

## Lateral movement

Crab walk — move side to side or with a cross-step. Never turn and go back. Options: zero steps (hold position), one step, or cross-step. The crab walk keeps your chest facing the net and your weight forward.

## When a slow ball arrives: create space

If a dink lands slow, don't just hit it passively from close range. **Shift laterally** to create space, then hit. If you want a forehand on the left side, shift left and open up. This gives you room to swing offensively.

## Elbow tucked for fast hands

In fast exchanges, keep the elbow tucked close to the body. This limits backswing and keeps the paddle in front. When elbow flies out, the paddle goes wide and you can't reset quickly. Elbow in = compact, fast-twitch paddle.

**Use the wrist for power at the net** (when ball is high). Not the whole arm and shoulder — just a snap of the wrist downward.

## Finish forward after attacking

After a speedup or aggressive shot, don't fall backward expecting to win the point. Stay leaning forward — ready for the cleanup shot. The opponent will often block it back.

## Shot Selection by Contact Height (4th Shot from NVZ)

When at the NVZ and opponents are transitioning in, shot choice depends on ball height:

**Forehand:**
- High above net → swinging volley
- At net level / slightly below → roll (low-to-high)
- Below knee → step back, attack off bounce at apex

**Backhand:**
- High above net → swinging volley (power, keep deep)
- Around net level → backhand flick (topspin + pace)
- Slightly below net but above knee → backhand roll (take early, topspin, time pressure)
- Below knee → step back, 2H backhand off bounce

**Never volley below knee level.** Create space, gather feet, attack off the bounce.

## Drive-Drop Counter (When Opponents Drive the 3rd)

1. Hit the deepest, most solid counter you can — don't overhit
2. Keep counters low even if they land shorter (forces a drop fifth)
3. After solid counter, lean in for a volley/flick/roll on the 5th
4. If you pop up a weak counter: don't lean in — hunker down, step back for time if needed

## Against Aggressive Topspin Drops

Three options when opponents crash forward with a topspin drop:
1. **Shuffle back + dink angle** to sideline — low, away from middle
2. **Come off line + safe ball** short to center — low + middle = limits angles
3. **Half-volley dink** (5.0+): reach in, decide too low, pull back, play safe to kitchen

Keep balls low, away from middle, away from waist height.

**When to concede the NVZ:** If the third shot is too good, step back and absorb. Better to retreat than force a high-contact ball from a weak position.
