# Kitchen (Non-Volley Zone)

The 7-foot area on each side of the net, extending the full width of the court. Players may not volley (hit the ball before it bounces) while touching the kitchen or its lines. The kitchen line is the most strategically important real estate in pickleball — controlling it is the single biggest differentiator between levels.

## The Rules

- No volleys while standing in the kitchen or touching the kitchen line
- Momentum carrying a player into the kitchen after a volley is also a violation (e.g., stepping in after contact counts)
- Players may enter the kitchen freely after the ball has bounced
- **2026 act of volleying clarification:** The "act of volleying" now formally begins when a player moves toward the ball with intention to volley, not only at the moment of contact

## Why the Kitchen Matters

**The kitchen is where points are won and lost.** At the kitchen line:
- Players who arrive first gain offensive positioning
- Balls below the net tape cannot be attacked — they must be dipped (reset or dropped)
- Balls above the net tape can be attacked — creating put-away opportunities
- Dink rallies at the kitchen are the central pattern of high-level play

**Getting to the kitchen is the tactical goal** of the third shot drop and transition game: the serving team must advance from the baseline to the kitchen against an already-positioned return team.

## Kitchen-Line Footwork

- **Crab walk:** Lateral shuffle, staying square to the net. Never turn your back to opponents at the kitchen line.
- **Wide base:** Feet wider than shoulder-width, knees bent, weight on balls of feet
- **"Cliff behind you"** mental cue: imagining a cliff behind the heels prevents the common error of drifting back from the line
- See [Footwork](footwork.md) for the full breakdown

## Grip at the Kitchen

Choke up on the paddle handle when stationed at the kitchen. The shorter lever arm speeds up hand exchanges. Ben Johns and Gabe Tardio both demonstrably choke up at the kitchen line. See [Grip](grip.md).

## Don't Rush the Kitchen (Mid-Court Discipline)

The counterintuitive insight from Mini (5.6, 20yrs coaching): **do not rush the kitchen.** The correct principle is to advance when "invited" — when the shot you just hit earns the advance. Rushing forward without a quality transition shot results in being caught mid-stride with a difficult ball to handle.

**Three steps (Mini / PPB):**
1. Wait until invited (Monitor: is the ball I hit a quality drop/reset that earns my advance?)
2. Only attack if you have time — time > height (a high ball arriving fast is not attackable)
3. Feet in position before contact — wide base, weight set
