# Lob Serve

A high-arc serve designed to land in the last 2–3 feet of the service box near the baseline. Popularized at the pro level by [Anna Leigh Waters](../entities/annalie-waters.md) in early 2026. The lob serve flips the normal serve/return dynamic — converting the server's inherently defensive position into an offensive one.

## Why It Works

Serving is normally a defensive position in pickleball — the return team has the advantage because they can rush the kitchen while the server waits for the bounce. The lob serve disrupts this by:

1. Forcing the receiver deep — the ball lands at or behind the baseline, not in mid-court
2. Delaying the receiver's kitchen approach by several seconds
3. Generating a weak, pop-up return that the server can attack
4. Creating confusion about where to take the ball (off the bounce vs. letting it reach peak height)

## Technique

1. **Setup:** Paddle face points toward the side fence. Arm relaxed at side, elbow slightly bent.
2. **Ball placement:** Extend the toss/drop hand in front of the body, into the strike zone.
3. **Swing path:** Down → out → up (same general shape as a normal serve).
4. **Key difference at contact:** Paddle face is open (not vertical like a normal serve). The face is tilted back toward you.
5. **Brushing motion:** Instead of driving through the ball, brush the paddle back toward your body after contact — a "flexing" motion ending with the arm in a bicep curl position. This generates backspin and height simultaneously.
6. **Height = effectiveness:** The higher the serve, the more time it takes for the ball to land, and the more dangerous the timing challenge for the receiver.

## Depth Requirement

The serve must land within the last 2–3 feet of the baseline to be effective. Anything shorter allows the receiver to step in, attack off the bounce, and rush forward. A shallow lob serve is worse than a normal serve — it gives the opponent an easy forehand attack near mid-court.

## Optimal Targeting: Odd Side to Odd Side

Anna Leigh uses the lob serve predominantly from the odd (left) side of the court targeting the odd (left) side of the opponent's court. The reason:

- The natural trajectory of a serve from the left side travels toward the opponent's left (backhand) side
- From the even (right) side, the trajectory goes toward the opponent's right (forehand) — easier to attack
- Targeting the backhand forces either a difficult backhand response or a run-around that pulls the receiver off-court and delays their kitchen approach further

## Against Stackers

The lob serve is most effective when opponents are [stacking](stacking.md). A stacking pair must not only return from deep in the court but also execute their positional swap — compounding the delay before either player reaches the kitchen.

## Counter: Take It Off the Bounce

The correct counter to the lob serve is to step in and take the ball at the rise (just after the bounce) at waist height. Letting the ball reach its peak and come back down pushes the receiver to the back fence, produces a leaning-back contact, and guarantees a late kitchen arrival. The key drill: practice with a partner feeding deep lobs and taking every return off the rise in the strike zone.

## Risk

If the serve lands shorter than the last 3 feet, opponents attack it easily. Jack Sock demonstrated this against Anna Leigh — one serve that landed mid-court was immediately crushed. The lob serve is a high-risk, high-reward weapon, not a default serve.
