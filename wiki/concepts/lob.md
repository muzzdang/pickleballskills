# Lob

A high-arc shot intended to pass over the opponent's heads and land deep near the baseline. The lob is dramatically underused in recreational pickleball and is one of the highest-value reset tools available to intermediate and advanced players.

## Types of Lobs

**Defensive lob:** Hit when stretched, off-balance, or under pressure. Trades a weak exchange for a complete rally reset — forces both opponents back to the baseline. Even a mediocre defensive lob buys time and repositions the point.

**Offensive lob:** Hit when the opponent is positioned at the kitchen and leaning forward, expecting a dink. A well-timed offensive lob over their non-dominant shoulder can win the point outright or generate a weak overhead response.

## When to Use

- **Any time you are under pressure** at recreational levels — Austin Hardy recommends lobbing far more than players typically do. The risk of a lob going out is much lower than the risk of a shanked forced shot under pressure.
- **When opponents are crowding the kitchen line** — players leaning too far forward are vulnerable to lobs over their heads
- **As a change-up in dink rallies** — unpredictability is valuable; mixing in a lob keeps opponents from settling into a dink rhythm

## Lob Serve

The lob is also a viable serve technique (see [Lob Serve](lob-serve.md)). Anna Leigh Waters popularized the pro-level lob serve in 2026. Key difference: a lob serve must land in the last 2–3 feet of the service box to be effective.

## Counter: Return Deep, Take Height off the Bounce

Against a lob, the correct response is:
1. Step back early — don't wait for the ball to peak and drop
2. If reachable, hit an overhead from as high a contact point as possible
3. If too deep for an overhead, take the ball off the bounce off the back wall / near the baseline, return deep, and reset the rally
