# Off-Ball Positioning

## Definition

What the **non-involved partner** does when the opponent and their partner are engaged in a firefight or exchange. Off-ball positioning determines whether the partner can clean up redirected balls and provide court coverage — described by [Richard Pickleball](../entities/richard-pickleball.md) as "50% of the game" that almost no one practices.

## Why It Matters

When you're the off-ball player, a misdirected redirect or a winning ball to your side of the court can either be a routine putaway or a ball hitting you unprepared. The difference is completely determined by your paddle position and court positioning before the ball comes to you.

If you're standing "like this" (paddle down, body casual), your partner cannot trust you to cover their side — they hesitate to attack aggressively because they feel exposed.

## Two Firefight Scenarios

### 1. Up-the-Line Firefight (Most Common)
Partner and diagonal opponent are in a direct exchange.

**What to do**:
- Point the **paddle tip at the opponent** in the firefight
- Take a half-step toward the middle of the court (cut off redirect angles)
- Weight forward, engaged — never casually watching
- From this position, any redirect to body or middle can be handled with a short motion; any redirect to the far side (your sideline) is acceptable loss — let it go out or cover with a short backhand movement
- Poaching is secondary to cutting off the middle — the most common redirect is to center, not across the court

**Specific positions by handedness**:
- Left-side player: can choose paddle-neutral (covers forehand or backhand), or sit backhand/forehand based on preference — all valid from the ready position
- Right-side player: typically sits backhand-heavy; forehead coverage is secondary

### 2. Crosscourt Firefight (Left-Left Pattern)
Partner (on the left side) and opponent (left side) are engaged in a crosscourt exchange. You are on the right side, technically the "off-ball" player but directly in the line of crosscourt traffic.

**What to do**:
- **Pack the middle** — creep toward the center of the court as the crosscourt exchange continues
- Your partner's backhand is aimed crosscourt; they are vulnerable to balls toward the right-center
- By packing the middle, you take that vulnerability away
- The result: both players hold backhand coverage across the center — the right sideline is the only open gap, but any ball redirected there is likely to go wide
- Lefty partner: can stay neutral or mirror the same backhand-heavy middle pack

## Key Mistakes to Avoid

- **Paddle hanging down or at your side** — you can't counter, reset, or reach quickly from this position
- **Standing on the sideline with your body** — opens up the middle entirely; opponents will exploit the gap on any redirect
- **Watching the firefight passively** — staying flat-footed prevents any quick lateral movement when the ball comes

## Paddle Position Shortcut

Richard's universal recommendation: paddle tip pointed at the opponent in the firefight. From this neutral position you can slide backhand *or* forehand with equal ease — it is the most flexible ready position without requiring you to commit to one side.
