# Paddle Tracking

## Definition

The practice of watching the **opponent's paddle position** before and during their contact — rather than tracking the ball — in order to anticipate shot direction and height at close kitchen distances. A key pro-vs-amateur differentiator.

## Why It Works

At kitchen distance (~10 feet net-to-net), the reaction window is less than 4/10 of a second — not enough time to both track the ball AND position the paddle correctly. By reading the opponent's paddle *before* they hit, you buy extra reaction time and can pre-position your paddle in the correct zone.

## Reading the Paddle

| Opponent's Paddle Position | What It Means | Your Response |
|---|---|---|
| Raised above chest | Attack (drive down) incoming | Lower your paddle to defend feet |
| Neutral / hip height | Flat or roll incoming | Stay in ready position, prepare for medium pace |
| Dropped below waist | Must hit upward | Raise paddle, prepare to attack the pop-up |

The key insight: **do the opposite of what the opponent's paddle is doing**. If their paddle is high (about to hit down), yours goes low. If theirs is low (about to hit up), yours goes high.

## When to Use It

Most valuable in:
- Kitchen line exchanges and firefights
- Transition zone exchanges
- Any situation where you have reduced reaction time due to proximity

Ball tracking (watching the ball all the way through contact) is still the priority on baseline shots — there is enough time and the extra margin is worth it.

## Common Mistake

Reverting to ball tracking during a speed-up exchange because "watch the ball" is the deeply ingrained habit from all other racket sports. Requires conscious practice to override.
