# Reset Mechanics

A reset is the defensive response to a hard speedup or drive — redirecting the ball softly back into the kitchen rather than fighting it. The key mental shift: **stop fighting the ball, start directing it**.

## The three common mistakes

1. **Too big a backswing** — you don't have time; elbow flailing creates excessive swing
2. **Flinching / pulling away** — fear of getting hit causes the paddle to retreat at contact, sending the ball up
3. **Wrong paddle face** — using an open (dink) face on a reset pops the ball up; resets need a **neutral/square** face

## Correct mechanics

**Paddle position:** Out in front and away from body, in ready position. The paddle should **meet the ball**, not the ball meet the paddle. After you hit a shot, follow your paddle in the direction of the shot — you'll already be in reset position when the speedup comes.

**Elbow tucked:** Keep the elbow close to the body. A flailing elbow forces a big backswing. Elbow in = backswing automatically shortened.

**Square paddle face:** Not open like for a dink. A flat/neutral face redirects the ball low and straight. Open face = pop-up.

**Absorb, don't fight:** No swing, no counterattack — just block. The ball's pace does the work; your job is to redirect it downward with a still, stable paddle. Get into a low athletic stance (hard to flinch or be pushed off-balance when your center of gravity is low).

## The goal of a reset

Return a **low ball** that forces the opponent to hit up. Once they have to hit up, they lose their attack. You've neutralized the point.

## Against bangers

You can't stop a banger by hitting harder back. You stop them by giving them a low ball that forces them to stop driving. "You can't fight a banger — redirect them."

## Progressive drill

1. Partner feeds at 20% pace — block it back
2. Increase to 50%, then 70%, then 100%
3. Each time you reset successfully, partner increases pace
