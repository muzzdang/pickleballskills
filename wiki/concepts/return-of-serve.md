# Return of Serve

The return team's response to the serve. Returning is a position of advantage in pickleball — the return team can immediately advance to the kitchen, while the serving team must wait for the return to bounce before they can approach. Maximizing this advantage starts with a quality return.

## The Return Advantage

The serving team is on defense structurally: they served, so they must let the return bounce (double bounce rule). This gives the return team time to rush the kitchen while the servers are frozen. A good return extends this gap; a short return compresses it.

## Key Principles

**Depth is mandatory (Rule 1 — Ben Johns / PPB):**
The return must land deep — within 3 feet of the baseline. A shallow return gives the serving team an easy mid-court ball they can attack or drive, neutralizing the return team's advantage. Deep returns push servers back and force a difficult third shot.

**Height adds time:**
A return with height (a higher arc) takes longer to land, giving the return team more time to reach the kitchen. A flat, low return travels faster and gives the servers less time but also leaves a smaller margin for error.

**Deep and high = maximum advantage.** The return that gives the returning team the most time to reach the kitchen is one that is both deep and somewhat high — it lands at the baseline and gives the servers a difficult high contact point.

## Against the Lob Serve

The lob serve disrupts the normal return dynamic by forcing the receiver deep. Counter strategy:
- Step in to meet the ball at the rise (just after the bounce) in the strike zone (below waist)
- Do not let the ball reach its peak — letting it peak pushes you to the back fence and forces a leaning-back contact
- Focus on returning deep and high to buy time for the kitchen approach
- See [Lob Serve](lob-serve.md)

## 4th Shot (Return Team's Second Shot)

Ben Johns's Rule 1 applies to both the return AND the fourth shot — depth is required on both. After the return, the return team will hit a fourth shot (after the serving team's third shot bounce) — this fourth shot should also be deep (within 3 feet of baseline) to keep the servers pinned back. A shallow fourth shot allows the servers to attack from mid-court.
