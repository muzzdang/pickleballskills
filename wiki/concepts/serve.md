# Serve

The serve initiates every rally in pickleball. Unlike tennis, pickleball serving begins from a defensive position — the serving team must wait for the ball to bounce before advancing, while the return team can rush the kitchen immediately. Effective serving minimizes this disadvantage and sets up favorable return patterns.

## 2026 Rule Updates

- Serve motion must produce an upward arc at contact
- The highest point of the paddle head must not exceed the wrist at contact
- Spin serves via finger manipulation (pre-contact spin) remain banned
- Net post contact: a serve that hits the net post and lands in the correct service court is now in play
- Timeout rule: timeouts may be called before the score is called; once announced, the rally is live

## Mechanics: Maximum Serve Speed (Austin Hardy / PPB — 7 Steps)

1. **Bounce the ball:** Bouncing before tossing gets the arm rhythm started naturally
2. **Elbow back:** Pull the elbow back during the wind-up (not just the paddle)
3. **C-shaped swing arc:** Elbow leads back, then the paddle head whips in a curved path — not a flat pendulum. This is the single largest contributor to serve speed.
4. **Elbow drives inward at contact:** Generates forearm pronation at the contact moment
5. **Stand at contact:** Legs straighten to full height at contact, raising contact point and adding vertical force
6. **Full shoulder rotation:** Non-paddle shoulder drives through; not an arm-only serve
7. **Jump/push off back foot:** Natural follow-through pushes the server forward, beginning transition movement simultaneously

**Mental cue:** Think "slap" not "push." Slap activates faster wrist snap and natural pronation.

## Lob Serve

Anna Leigh Waters popularized the lob serve at the pro level in early 2026. See [Lob Serve](lob-serve.md) for full breakdown. Key differences from a normal serve:
- Open paddle face at contact
- Brushing motion back toward the body (not driving through)
- Must land in last 2–3 feet of baseline to be effective
- Most effective targeting the opponent's backhand (odd side to odd side)

## Serve Strategy

- **Deep is non-negotiable:** Austin's Rule 10 from Ben Johns: "Serve deep, not just in." A shallow serve gives the opponent an easy mid-court attack.
- **Change up:** Anna Leigh mixes fast-deep, fast-short, and lob serves to prevent the receiver from settling into a rhythm
- **Target the backhand:** The vast majority of recreational and pro players have a weaker backhand return. A deep serve to the backhand limits attack options.
- **vs. stackers:** When opponents are stacking, a well-placed lob serve (especially odd-side to odd-side) compounds the positional disruption
