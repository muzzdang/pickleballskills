# Slice Return

## What It Is

A return of serve hit with backspin — the paddle face is open and moves on a high-to-low, chopping path through the ball. The ball travels with underspin, stays low after the bounce, and decelerates rather than accelerating.

Associated with Mari Humberg, who hits more slice shots than almost any other pro and uses it on nearly 100% of her returns in women's doubles.

## Why Use It

**The slice buys time.** The ball decelerates on its way to the server, giving the returning team extra time to advance toward the kitchen. This is the primary reason to slice on the return — not spin, not placement.

**Ball type matters:**
- **Franklin balls** (lower bounce) → slice is more effective; server must get very low to dig it up, and the ball takes longer to fall.
- **Lifetime balls** (higher bounce) → slightly less advantage.

## When NOT to Slice

Spin continuation is the risk. When a slice return lands in front of the server, they can drive with topspin — that topspin accelerates in the same direction as their swing, making the ball easier to control and harder for you to read.

- **Against players who hit strong topspin thirds** → slice return makes their job easier (spin continuation).
- **Mixed doubles at high levels** → men's third-shot drives benefit from your underspin; lean toward flat or topspin returns instead.
- **Women's doubles** → slice almost every time (opponents typically hitting drops, not topspin drives).

The rule: slice when opponents won't hit aggressive topspin thirds off of it. If they do, the advantage reverses.

## The Four Teaching Points (Mari Humberg)

### 1. Split Step
Trigger: **as the server contacts the ball**. Not before, not after. That's the cue to split. The split step puts you in an athletic, mobile position before you know where the ball is going.

### 2. Unit Turn
Turn the **entire body** to the ball side — not just the arm. Hips and shoulders rotate together. The non-unit turn (arm without body) is the most common mistake for recreational players, especially those without a tennis background. You can't generate body-driven power without the unit turn.

### 3. Meet the Ball
Go to the ball. Don't wait for it to come to you. Forward momentum at contact, with a moment of stillness at the actual strike, then continue moving forward toward the kitchen. Waiting for the ball forces you to hit while stationary or running — both are less consistent.

### 4. Contact Out in Front
If the ball reaches beside or behind your body before contact, the paddle face opens and the ball pops up. Contact in front = face angle controlled = consistent backspin. "The closer the ball is to your body, the easier it is to lose control."

## Technique

**The slice is not a wrist shot.** Lock the wrist completely. The backspin is generated by:
- Paddle face angle (open face — angled toward the sky)
- High-to-low chopping path through the ball
- Body drive (unit turn, forward momentum)

Not by wrist flip. Using the wrist produces inconsistent face angle and gives you a "tennis-looking" shot that won't work consistently in pickleball.

**Attack the ball, don't push it.** Commitment is key. A casual push produces a ball that floats up. A committed attack with locked wrist and body drive produces a ball that stays low and has real backspin. If you stop your body and overuse the arm, you overhit — go full with the body and let the swing control the pace.

**Pace control:** If opponents are hitting quality shots off your slice, slow down — same technique, less pace. You'll get more time to reach the kitchen and the ball will still stay low.

## Common Errors

| Error | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Ball pops up | Late contact (ball beside/behind body) | Meet the ball; contact out in front |
| Ball floats | Using wrist instead of face angle | Lock wrist; let face and path create spin |
| Lost control on arm swing | Stopped body, arm-only swing | Go full with body rotation |
| No backspin | Pushing rather than attacking | Commit to the chop; knife the ball |
