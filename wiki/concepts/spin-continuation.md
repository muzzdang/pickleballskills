# Spin Continuation

Use your opponent's spin against them by applying the **opposite** spin on your return. This neutralizes the incoming rotation and makes the shot dramatically easier to control.

## How it works

When topspin travels across the net to you, it arrives rotating the same direction as backspin from your perspective. So:

| Opponent hits | Looks like from your side | Your response |
|---------------|--------------------------|---------------|
| Topspin | Backspin arriving | Hit backspin back |
| Backspin / slice | Topspin arriving | Hit topspin back |

The physics: if you try to add topspin onto an already-topspin ball, you're fighting the rotation. If you use the opposite spin, you're going with the existing rotation.

## Reading spin

Two cues:
1. **Watch paddle motion** — low-to-high = topspin outgoing; high-to-low or under the ball = slice/backspin
2. **Watch the ball in flight** — visible rotation if spin is heavy enough

## The common mistake

Trying to rip topspin back against a heavy topspin serve or ball. The counter-rotation forces create inconsistency. Use backspin (slice) instead.

## When opponent hits no spin

Flat/no-spin ball is a blank canvas — apply whatever spin you want. Topspin is generally recommended as the default.

## Slice Return Risk (Return of Serve Context)

When the returning team hits a slice return, the server receives the ball with backspin. If the server then drives with topspin (spin continuation), the topspin accelerates in the same direction as their swing — making the drive easier to control and harder for the returning team to handle.

This is why:
- Slice returns are risky against opponents who hit strong topspin third-shot drives
- Slice returns are more effective against opponents who drop (no drive = no spin continuation acceleration)
- Mari Humberg slices in women's doubles (opponents typically drop) but uses more flat returns in mixed doubles (men drive more with topspin)
