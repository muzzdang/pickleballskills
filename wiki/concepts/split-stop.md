# Split Stop

A fundamental footwork move: a small hop or step that widens the feet and lowers the center of gravity, timed so you land and are stationary *before* your opponent makes contact with the ball.

## The critical timing rule

Split stop **before** your opponent contacts the ball — not after.

The common mistake: waiting until the opponent hits, then split stopping. By then, you're wasting time completing the hop while the ball is already in the air. Split early so you're already balanced and ready to push off the moment you read the ball direction.

## Why it works

- Lowers center of gravity → more stable contact
- Enables any direction of movement (shuffle left, right, forward, backward) from a balanced base
- Allows your body to generate force behind the shot rather than relying only on the arm

## In the transition zone

Split stop as your opponent is about to strike so you're ready to push off toward wherever they direct the ball. In the third-shot-drop approach sequence: hit drop → gather ground → split stop → hit another drop → repeat until you reach the kitchen.

## Timing cue

Watch for the moment your opponent is "about to swing" — split just before the paddle-ball contact, not after you see the ball leave their paddle.

**On the return of serve:** The split step triggers on server contact specifically. As soon as the server makes contact with the ball, that's the cue — split immediately, then unit turn and move to the ball. This is the first step of the slice return sequence. See: [Slice Return](slice-return.md)

## Drills

Any drill where you're moving through the court: practice stopping and lowering before every opposing contact. It becomes automatic with repetition.
