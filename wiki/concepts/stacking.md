# Stacking

A doubles formation strategy where both players stand on the same side of the court before the serve or return, then swap positions as soon as the ball is struck. Stacking allows teams to keep their preferred players on their preferred sides regardless of score rotation.

## Why Teams Stack

In standard pickleball rotation, the serve and return alternate sides based on the score (even score = right side, odd score = left side). For teams with a strong forehand-dominant player who prefers the left side (to protect the middle with their forehand), rotation can frequently put them on the wrong side. Stacking eliminates this problem.

**Classic example:** A mixed doubles team with a stronger male partner — the male prefers the left side (more active, covers middle with his forehand). Stacking lets him always play the left side regardless of score.

## How Stacking Works

**Serving team stack:**
- Both players start on the same side of the court (right or left of the centerline)
- Server hits from the correct box (based on score)
- After the serve, players sprint to their preferred sides before the return lands

**Receiving team stack:**
- The returner starts in the correct box
- Their partner stands behind them on the same side, outside the court boundary
- After the return is struck, both players move to their preferred sides

## Vulnerability: The Lob Serve Exploit

Stacking teams are uniquely vulnerable to the [lob serve](lob-serve.md). When a stacking returner must receive a deep lob serve, they are:
1. Pulled far back and off-court to handle the deep ball
2. Still needing to execute their positional swap with their partner

Both events happening simultaneously creates severe delays in reaching the kitchen — worse than the standard lob-serve delay against a non-stacking team.

[Anna Leigh Waters](../entities/annalie-waters.md) specifically uses the lob serve more often against stacking opponents for this reason.
