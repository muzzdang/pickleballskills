# Third Shot Drive

A hard groundstroke hit as the third shot (after the serve return), used when the serving team is pushed behind the baseline and a drop isn't viable. The goal is to force a short reply, not to win the point outright.

## When to drive vs. drop

- Feet **behind the baseline** → drive
- Feet **inside the baseline** → [third shot drop](third-shot-drop.md) is preferred

From behind the baseline, the margin for a drop is too small. A drive forces the opponent to block, which typically produces a shorter reply — then approach and drop from there (fifth shot drop).

## Technique

**Early prep:** Take the paddle back *before* moving to the ball — never move first and prep second. Body sideways, paddle back, then go to the ball.

**Turn away from body:** When taking the paddle back, hold it away from the body (think "hug a tree" / "leave room for the Holy Ghost"). Turning with paddle tight to body creates jammed contact.

**Contact out in front:** In line with front knee; extended contact. This is especially important when moving *up* through the transition zone — turn your body sideways before hitting, even when moving forward.

**Head down through contact:** Keep head down and forward at, during, and after contact; pick it up after the ball is gone. Lifting the head early pulls the body up and sends the ball long.

**60-70% power:** Not 100%. A slower drive is more consistent, lands lower, and produces a better short reply. The goal is to get the opponent to dig the ball — a lower, controlled drive achieves that better than a ball sailing long.

## After the drive

**Move forward immediately.** The goal of the drive is a short reply. Know this, expect it, and start moving forward the instant you strike the ball. Having the ball bounce twice because you stayed back = wrong mindset.

**Follow your shot.** Move toward where the ball went, not straight up the middle. This keeps you in the right coverage position.

**Drive → Drop, never Drive → Drive.** After the drive, transition to a [third shot drop](third-shot-drop.md) as you advance. Hitting two drives in a row means you're sprinting forward without dropping — you get caught running and lose position.

**[Split stop](split-stop.md)** when the opponent is about to contact your drive. Advance, split, advance, split.

## Drill

Drive → drop → drive → drop (alternating). Builds muscle memory for the correct post-drive behavior.

## When to Drive (Decision Framework)

The drive/drop decision is primarily about **opponent position**, not ball height:
- **Opponent back / not established** → drive
- **Opponent up and established at kitchen** → drop (driving into an established kitchen is a "death sentence")

**By return depth:** Deep return (you're behind the baseline) → almost always drive. The kitchen is tiny from back there; a drive gets you to the net in 1-2 shots vs. 3+ on a drop attempt.

**By spin:** Slice return → lean toward driving. Spin continuation: slice → your drive creates topspin → ball dips hard at opponent. Aim higher than normal (shoulder level) and it will still dip to their feet.

**Pace:** 70% from behind baseline; 40-50% from mid-court. From mid-court you're guiding the ball, not generating pace.

See the full framework: [Drive vs. Drop Decision](drive-vs-drop-decision.md)

## Floor Variant: The Mush Drive

When you're tight, in a big match, or just not feeling your normal drive: use the **mush drive**.

- **No swing.** Paddle starts out in front of the body — no backswing.
- **Push forward** to opponent's chest/body. Aim high (body level), not one inch over the net.
- Zero variables = near-100% make rate. You will not miss this ball.
- Easier to time up on fast-incoming returns than a full swing.
- The trade-off: you won't get the pace of a regular drive, but the opponent's volley will come back short and easy — which sets up the push drop perfectly.
