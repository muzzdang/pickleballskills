# Third Shot Drop

The most important shot unique to pickleball. A soft arc shot hit by the serving team after the return, designed to land in or near the opponent's kitchen and allow the serving team to advance toward the net. **The goal is not to win the point — it's to advance safely.**

## When to Drop (Decision Framework)

The core trigger is **opponent position**:
- **Opponent established at kitchen** → drop
- **One foot inside baseline + can lean forward through contact** → drop

**By spin:** Topspin return favors dropping. Spin continuation: topspin return → your drop gets underspin → pillowy, consistent arc. "Way more consistent with a topspin return."

**Slice return:** Lean toward driving instead. Dropping a slice reliably requires near-perfect mechanics and is low percentage at most levels.

**Concede when necessary:** If the opponent is up and established and you don't have a clear drive, concede the kitchen — hit a dink, let them come in, reset, and create offense from neutral.

See the full framework: [Drive vs. Drop Decision](drive-vs-drop-decision.md)

## When to drop vs. when to drive

Use feet position as the trigger:
- **Feet inside the baseline** → third shot drop
- **Feet behind the baseline** → third shot drive instead (see [third-shot-drive](third-shot-drive.md))

Hitting a drop from behind the baseline shrinks the margin dramatically. A drive from there forces the opponents to block, giving you a higher ball closer in — then drop from there (fifth shot drop).

## Technique

**Paddle face is everything.** At the point of contact, paddle face must point toward the target. A closed face sends it into the net; an open face pops it up.

**Short backswing.** Keep the paddle in front of your body. Big backswings = inconsistent face angle at contact. Natural topspin will occur from the low-to-high motion; you don't need to force it.

**You don't need heavy topspin.** A backhand slice drop with backspin is still a good drop. The direction and height matter more than spin. Don't let the pursuit of perfect topspin create an unreliable big swing.

**Match pace inversely.** Opponent hits hard → soften your touch (they did the work). Opponent hits soft → add a bit more pace (or it dies in the net). Don't match what they give you.

**Aim at feet/shins, not the net tape.** Give yourself margin over the net. A ball landing at your opponent's feet is effective even if it bounces slightly high — it still limits their attack options. Trying to land it millimeters over the net from deep in the court is extremely low-percentage.

## Body mechanics: back-to-front, stay low

**Weight transfers back to front** — the most common error is "arming it," hitting the drop with the arm only and no body. The back foot loads and the weight drives forward through contact.

**Stay compressed** — the body stays low throughout the swing. Rising up through the shot is the cause of popped-up drops. The shoulder drives *forward and down*, not up.

**Foot behind the ball** — get the trail foot directly behind the ball's landing zone before swinging. Tennis players default to sliding laterally to the side (wrong in pickleball). Foot behind → paddle in front → consistent contact in "the pocket."

**The pocket** — Cori Elliott's term for the consistent ready-position contact zone in front of the body. Return the paddle to the same spot every time so you're finding the ball rather than reaching.

## Step back after serving

After hitting your serve, take one step back off the baseline. Returners aim deep — you want room to move *into* the drop rather than having to back up and hit simultaneously. Backing up while striking is the cause of most T-Rex arm / inconsistent contact.

## Advancing to the kitchen

**Don't sprint.** After hitting the drop, gather ground but split step before your opponent makes contact. Work forward in cycles: drop → split → advance → drop again. This is a marathon, not a sprint. Sprinting leads to getting caught running while the ball hits your body.

The [split step](split-stop.md) must happen before the opponent strikes — not after.

## Floor Variant: The Push Drop

When your regular drop isn't landing (no feel, wind, tight moments): use the **push drop**.

- Open the paddle face, lift at a **45-degree angle**, follow through *forward* not up.
- No wrist. No swing. It's a controlled lift from the legs.
- Works on both forehand and backhand wings.
- Pairs with the [mush drive](third-shot-drive.md#floor-variant-the-mush-drive) — drive the ball (no swing), their volley comes back short, push drop it in.

> *"Literally bang. Paddle face open, no wrist action, through — not up and not out, through like a 45-degree angle."* — Richard Pickleball
