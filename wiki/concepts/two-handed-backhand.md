# Two-Handed Backhand

A backhand technique using both hands on the paddle — borrowed from tennis, where the two-handed backhand is standard. In pickleball, the two-handed backhand is increasingly used at the kitchen for dink rolls and at mid-court for drives, though it requires specific adaptations from the tennis version due to the shorter court and underhand ball heights.

## Two-Handed Backhand Dink / Roll Dink (Luke Larsen / PPB)

The "two-handed backhand roll dink" — taught by Luke Larsen (5.5, 25 years experience) — is an offensive dink variant using topspin to create a low, dipping ball at the opponent's feet.

### Grip
- **Paddle hand:** Continental grip. This keeps the face open enough to generate the low-to-high brush for topspin.
- Not an eastern or western — those close the face too much for the required upward brush

### Grip Pressure
- **Light throughout.** Luke's primary cue: grip the paddle as lightly as possible while maintaining control.
- Heavy pressure stiffens the wrist → kills the topspin brush → ball goes long or into the net
- The "broken wrist" feeling throughout the swing indicates correct lightness

### Contact Point
- **Inside the left knee** (for right-handed players)
- Not out in front of the body and not to the side — inside the left knee, in a "V-stance" created by the feet
- This contact position creates consistent depth and angle

### Swing Mechanics
- **Low-to-high:** Paddle starts below the ball, brushes upward through contact
- **Shoulder-driven:** The rotation comes from the shoulder, not the arm pushing forward
- **Non-dominant hand** provides stability and power through the follow-through
- **Follow the ball:** Paddle face follows the ball's path for 6–8 inches after contact. This prevents the flick/wrist-flip error that sends the ball long.

### V-Stance
Feet form a V shape at contact — left foot forward, right foot back, weight flowing toward the net through the swing.

## Anna Leigh Waters and the Two-Handed BH

[Anna Leigh Waters](../entities/annalie-waters.md) is one of the most prominent users of the two-handed backhand at the pro level. However, Austin Hardy notes (via Hayden Patriquin breakdown) that her two-handed backhand has a vulnerability at balls directed at her right hip — forcing an awkward swing with the elbow in an inefficient position.
