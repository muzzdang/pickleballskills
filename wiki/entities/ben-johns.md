# Ben Johns

**Type:** Professional pickleball player
**Nationality:** American
**Known for:** Dominant hands battles; controlled forehand counter in continental grip; brother of Colin Johns; frequently featured in highlight firefight compilations

## Playing Style

Ben Johns is cited repeatedly as a model for controlled technique at the kitchen. [Richard Pickleball](../entities/richard-pickleball.md) specifically names him as someone who executes the **ping-pong style forehand counter** well — unit-turn shoulder motion with a preset wrist, avoiding the wrist-slap pattern most continental-grip players struggle with.

Appears in firefight highlight footage with compact, redirecting paddle work rather than large swings. Commentators note his ability to be "incredibly controlled" even in the fastest exchanges.

## Ben Johns' 10 Rules to Win (via PPB)

Austin Hardy analyzed Ben's strategy across tournament footage and distilled 10 rules:
1. Always cover the middle
2. Hit to the player in transition (not the established player)
3. Use the **bait ball** — hit soft to invite a predictable attack, then counter
4. Take every attackable ball
5. Reset when off-balance
6. Keep depth on drives
7. Partner mirrors your movement (string theory)
8. Attack the **body** in hands battles, not open court
9. Move forward after every drop
10. Serve deep, not just in

## Strategic Tendencies

- **String theory:** Ben and his partner move as if connected by a string — when one moves left, the other mirrors right, eliminating middle gaps
- **Choke up at the kitchen:** PPB confirms Ben demonstrably chokes up on the handle at the kitchen line, consistent with the zone-based grip principle
- **Middle with a forehand:** Ben prefers taking the middle with his forehand, often poaching his partner's ball when his forehand is the better shot
- **Vulnerability:** Hayden Patriquin and Anna Bright defeated Ben and Anna Leigh Waters in a notable match by targeting the middle exclusively — exposing communication uncertainty between the partnership

## Ready Position (PPB Level 2)

Austin Hardy uses Ben's ready position as the "Level 2" benchmark for intermediate players: paddle angled slightly open at waist height, elbow bent in front of body, weight slightly forward on balls of the feet. More responsive than a beginner's high-chest position.
