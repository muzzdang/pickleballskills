# How to Hit a Forehand Putaway Volley (Universal Rackets)

**Source:** YouTube
**Channel:** [Universal Rackets](../entities/universal-rackets.md)

## Key Takeaways

- **Hit through three balls, not one.** The core cue: instead of stopping at contact, imagine swinging through three consecutive balls in a line. Finish in the center of the body — paddle finishes forward, not across the body — to maintain balance and readiness for the counter.
- **Tuck the elbow; use the wrist.** Keeping the elbow tucked into the body eliminates the oversized arm swing that causes shots to sail wide. The power source is the wrist, not the shoulder.
- **Close the paddle face on high balls.** When the ball is above net height the paddle face must tilt downward so the ball goes into the court. The lower the ball, the more open the face; the higher the ball, the more closed. Players commonly keep the same open dinking grip on a high ball and hit it out.
- **Paddle out front; don't let the ball jam you.** Start with the paddle extended forward before the ball arrives. This creates space for an aggressive swing. Taking the paddle back behind the wrist — not behind the body — is fine, but the contact must happen out in front.
- **Load and explode from the legs; shift sideways, not back.** Dropping the center of gravity allows swinging more forward (rather than downward), increasing aggression from lower contact points. When covering the middle, shift laterally — never step backward — to keep body weight moving into the shot.
- **Aim big; give yourself margin.** Rather than painting the line, aim at the inner half of the target zone. Wherever the paddle finishes, the ball follows: finish crosscourt to go crosscourt; wait one extra millisecond and finish down-the-line to go DTL.
- **"I would rather have you miss long going for it than tap the ball back."** Tyler's philosophy: a half-hearted tap tells opponents they can pop up with impunity.

## Visual Notes

Transcript describes Tyler demonstrating the finish position: paddle ends up center-front of the body (not across), elbows tucked, body in a slight squat (loaded legs). The "pretend there's a wall in front and behind you" cue prevents the common back-stepping error during aggressive shots.
