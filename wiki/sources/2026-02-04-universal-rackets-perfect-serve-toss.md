## Key Takeaways

- **Call it the "serve drop," not the "serve toss."** This reframing eliminates the instinct to toss the ball upward (from tennis). In pickleball, the palm faces downward — you release the ball, you don't push it up. Tossing up creates timing variability and wind susceptibility.
- **Arm fully extended, then drop at 1:00 (righty) or 11:00 (lefty).** "Snow angel" arm — straight out to the side. The 1:00 position positions the ball slightly forward and to the side, which allows rotation and puts contact out in front. Straight ahead (12:00) = gets jammed; too wide (3:00) = comes around the ball.
- **Release height: around hip height, slightly above.** Releasing too high gives wind time to affect the ball and forces you to wait. Releasing too low means you can't get under the ball for a proper low-to-high swing (which is required for spin and consistency). Drop from around hip/snow-angel height — not above, not at the knee.
- **Just release — don't slam it down.** Treat it like an arcade claw machine: position, then release. No wrist flick downward, no movement — gravity does the work. Any spin visible on the ball means you used unnecessary wrist on the drop.
- **Keep the tossing arm up after release.** The non-hitting arm going down causes the body to open early, ruining rotation and power. Keep the non-dominant arm elevated and to the side during the swing — it naturally helps keep the body turned. Finish with both hands touching (non-dominant hand catching the follow-through paddle).
- **Tossing arm pointing upward after release = rotation cue.** As the hitting arm swings, the tossing arm should be rising. "Lift both arms up like you're throwing a medicine ball." Both arms lift together.
- **Air serves vs. bounce serves.** Bounce serves create a ceiling on your development — you can't generate spin, power, or variety reliably. Drop-and-catch drill (no paddle) 1000 times at 1:00 to build the toss habit before adding the swing.

## Visual Notes

Tyler demonstrates the arcade claw metaphor: arm out, position ball, let it drop straight down (gravity only). Contrasts this with the common fault of slamming the ball downward. Shows the non-dominant arm rising after the drop — keeping both arms coordinated.
