# Stubborn Pickleball Habits Losing You Matches (Universal Rackets)

**Source:** YouTube
**Channel:** [Universal Rackets](../entities/universal-rackets.md)

**Uploaded:** 2026-02-13

## Key Takeaways

Tyler and Michele co-host. Specific bad habits called out with corrections (some match predictions, many do not):

1. **No ready position.** After hitting, paddle drops down. Many players don't know what a ready position is. Fix: after every shot, touch the paddle with the non-dominant hand to force the return to ready position. Drill: slow dinks, touch after every hit. The non-dominant hand also automatically guides the swing upward (hands up = natural lift angle).
2. **Not knowing how to transition forehand ↔ backhand.** Players get stuck in forehand grip and reach around for wide balls, or stay in backhand and miss forehands. Fix: "the paddle center of body is a magnet" — always return to neutral ready position after each shot. The ball's position relative to the center of your body tells you forehand or backhand.
3. **Watching the shot instead of following the ball.** After hitting, players freeze and admire (or lament) their shot. The ball is coming back. Fix: follow the ball with your body. If you hit crosscourt, move crosscourt. If you drove down the middle, follow it. Don't run straight ahead automatically.
4. **Wrong paddle tilt.** (a) Incorrect grip entirely — hand up the handle, eliminating leverage. (b) Same paddle face angle for all contact heights — open face for high balls sends them out; closed face for low balls sends them into the net. Fix: low contact = open face; high contact = closed face.
5. **Running with paddle down.** When transitioning from baseline to kitchen, paddle should rise as you approach — not stay down. The closer to the kitchen, the higher the paddle. Near baseline: lower (balls bounce lower there). At kitchen: paddle up, ready for fast exchanges. 
6. **Not communicating with partner.** Silence causes middle-ball miscommunication and vibe death. Fix: say "mine" or "yours" even for obvious balls. Ask at the start: "Do you prefer forehand or backhand in the middle?" A quiet partner isn't a bad player — they're probably nervous.
7. **Panic after popping the ball up.** Two mistakes: (a) turning away and giving up — but pickleball's designed to come back, even from high balls; (b) being so afraid of popping up that you hit into the net instead. Fix: if you pop it up, stay in the point and defend. Roger Federer lost half his points too — it's about how you react.
8. **Paddle low when moving up to the kitchen.** Same as running with paddle down — players drive and then their paddle goes down as they come in. Fix: move with paddle up and pointed toward the play, so any short blocked ball is immediately reachable.
