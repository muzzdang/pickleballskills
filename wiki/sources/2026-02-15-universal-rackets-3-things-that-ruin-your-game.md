# 3 Things Pickleball Players Do That Ruin Their Game (Universal Rackets)

**Source:** YouTube
**Channel:** [Universal Rackets](../entities/universal-rackets.md)

## Key Takeaways

Titled "3 Things" but actually contains more — a comprehensive dinking breakdown. The three primary problems from the transcript:

1. **Unnecessary swing / too much movement.** Dinking is a push from A to B — 6 inches of movement max. Any additional backswing, wrist, or shoulder motion adds uncontrollable variables to a very small target (the kitchen). Fix: do as little as possible. Think "push," not "hit." The drill: go out and try to get the ball over the net with the fewest paddle movements possible.
2. **Wrong paddle face.** Using the same closed/drive grip at the kitchen. When making contact below the net (most dinks), you need the paddle face open so the ball can be lifted upward. "Arrow out the center of your paddle — when low, arrow must point up." Opposite problem for high balls: same open grip → ball goes out. Close the face for high contact.
3. **Not moving feet (or moving feet wrong).** Beginners learned "take as little steps as possible" — correct at first to prevent falling into the kitchen. But at intermediate level, that turns into reaching rather than moving. Reaching = popping up. Fix: get your BODY behind the ball, THEN contact out in front and away. Weight on toes (not heels) — lean over the kitchen, not sit back.

- **Dink = "push," think "compact, concise, controlled."** Less is more. Biggest mistake is treating the dink like a hitting motion.
- **Recover to the center after every dink.** Tyler's own mistake in 2023: drilling crosscourt only, then getting speeded up down the middle. After every dink, recover to center of the court. Aim for opponent's feet — go straight at them with a slight chance of being above net height — their wheelhouse is at the sides, not straight at their feet.
- **Three targets when dinking:** (1) out wide to pull opponent off court; (2) middle to create miscommunication; (3) directly at opponent's body/feet — underused but highly effective.
- **Get low to swing forward.** Waist-bend → must swing up → pops up. Leg-bend → can swing forward → stays low. Even a slight leg bend makes a "night and day" difference.
- **Limit the wrist entirely.** Using the wrist on dinks creates inconsistency. Push with arm and shoulder (more specifically, the chest and shoulder).
