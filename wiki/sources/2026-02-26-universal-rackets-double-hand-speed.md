# Double Your Hand Speed in Pickleball (Universal Rackets)

**Source:** YouTube
**Channel:** [Universal Rackets](../entities/universal-rackets.md)

## Key Takeaways

The "one weird technique" turns out to be multiple positional and positional cues — hand speed is largely a positioning problem, not a reflexes problem:

- **Tip 1: Start and finish in the same position.** The main reason players feel slow: after they hit a ball, their paddle ends up somewhere different from where it started. Then the next ball comes and they have to move the paddle to position again — losing critical time. "Wherever I start, I finish. Wherever I finish, I start." Forehand → still in ready. Backhand → still in ready. No repositioning time = faster effective hand speed.
- **Tip 2: Use the wrist, not the arm and shoulder.** Dinking is shoulder-driven. Volleys are wrist-driven. Players learn shoulder motion for dinking and carry it to volleys, where they need rapid wrist snaps. Tuck elbow into body, hold paddle out, use ONLY the wrist. Much faster than swinging the shoulder.
- **Tip 3: Tuck the elbow.** "The moment my elbow goes outside my body is the moment I take an unnecessary big swing." Elbow flailing = wide arc = slow. Elbow in = compact arc = faster effective movement. Drill: put a pickleball under your armpit and try to hold it there while hitting volleys.
- **Tip 4: Lean forward / weight on toes.** Leaning forward = contacting the ball earlier and farther out in front. Being passive/leaning back = ball gets close, jammed, late. "The earlier and more forward you can be, the better your volley." Weight on toes enables this lean.
- **Tip 5: Know what is a forehand and what is a backhand.** Many players take unnecessary forehands from close to the body — they try to hit a forehand on balls that are coming at them or across their body. "80% of your shots in pickleball are a backhand." Anything close to the body or across = backhand. This eliminates a lot of awkward, late forehands.
- **Tip 6 (implied): 80% backhand.** Train yourself to default to backhand — it's the faster, more natural response to most incoming balls at the kitchen. The forehand is for balls clearly on the forehand side and away from the body.

## Visual Notes

Tyler demonstrates the elbow tuck with armpit pickleball drill. Shows the forehand vs. backhand range-of-motion comparison — everything toward the middle and body side of center = backhand. Demonstrates weight-on-toes vs. heels: lean forward and contact point is 2 feet further forward.
