# How to Hit Forehand and Backhand Volleys (Universal Rackets)

**Source:** YouTube
**Channel:** [Universal Rackets](../entities/universal-rackets.md)

**Duration:** 27:16
**Uploaded:** 2026-03-18

## Key Takeaways

- **The volley problem: players don't start in the right position, so they can't get there in time.** Paddle down or close to the body → when ball comes fast, they're late. Fix: paddle out in front and away from the body at ALL TIMES. "Airing out your armpits." "Hugging a tree." Paddle pre-positioned = ball can be met; not pre-positioned = ball meets you (jammed).
- **80% backhand rule for volleys.** Anything at or across the body toward the left (for righties) is a backhand. "Too many players try to hit forehand when the ball comes toward them." Taking unnecessary forehands from the body center = late, awkward, poppable. Default to backhand except for clear forehand side balls.
- **Block volley when attacked: squeeze paddle, open face, redirect.** When ball comes fast, no time for swing — squeeze the grip slightly (but not death grip), open the paddle face so the laser points up, and redirect the ball back at the attacker's feet. This is the block volley / reset from a volley position.
- **For high balls: snap the wrist down.** If ball is above net height and you're volleying, the wrist snap replaces the full swing. Elbow in, wrist from high to low — generates pace without a swing arc. "From here to here" — compact, fast.
- **For below-net offensive rolls: paddle tip down, finish up.** Same as offensive roll volley from the offensive masterclass — not a downward swing, but an upward brush with the paddle going tip-down to tip-up.
- **The full ready position system.** For dinks: weight on toes, lean forward, hold paddle out front. When someone speeds up: squeeze paddle, hold position (paddle pre-positioned means you don't need to move). Beat the attack by being already in position.
- **After a speed-up or volley exchange: immediately reset to ready position.** "Where I start is where I finish, where I finish is where I start." This eliminates repositioning time and appears to give faster hands.
