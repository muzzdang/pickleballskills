# I Fixed My Backhand Dink With 5 Simple Steps

**Source type:** YouTube video
**Channel:** [Pickleball Playbook](../entities/pickleballplaybook.md)
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LP-r4AyZBCQ
**Uploaded:** 2026-04-13

## Key Takeaways

- **Five body exercises to fix backhand dink:** Kieran diagnoses that Austin's backhand yip was a body mechanics problem, not a paddle problem. The five exercises target: (1) adductor release (inner thigh flexibility for wide stance); (2) outside leg stabilization (single-leg balance training); (3) foot loading — the "tripod" (weight distributed across three points: heel, ball of foot under big toe, ball of foot under pinky toe); (4) hip swivel (rotate hips through contact rather than only using arm); (5) knee tracking (knee must track over the same-side toe, not collapse inward).
- **Austin's yip was knee tracking + foot loading:** The root cause of Austin's backhand dink miss was that his back foot was rolling inward (collapsing arch), causing his knee to cave, destabilizing his hips, and producing an inconsistent arm path. Fixing foot position fixed the dink.
- **Tripod foot:** The most transferable cue — before every dink exchange, feel all three points of the foot contacting the ground. This activates the glutes and stabilizes the ankle-knee chain.
- **Hip swivel drives backhand:** The backhand dink should be initiated by the hip rotating (like a gentle rotation around a vertical axis), not by reaching the arm across the body. If the hip doesn't move, the arm overworks and goes inconsistent.
- **Athletic pre-load:** Kieran demonstrates an active "pre-load" stance — slightly bouncing on the balls of the feet before the dink exchange begins, staying kinetically ready rather than statically set.
