# Raise Your Floor FAST (Stop Playing Inconsistent Pickleball)

**Channel:** Richard Pickleball
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Mi7i1NS2vqQ
**Date:** 2026-04-24
**Duration:** 12:27

## Key Takeaways

- **Floor vs. ceiling** — your ceiling is the best you can play; your floor is the worst. Inconsistent players have a wide gap between the two. The goal isn't to raise the ceiling — it's to raise the floor. Ben Johns is the GOAT because his worst day is still a good day. He plays simple when he needs to.
- **The umbrella: simplicity** — both improvements (transition and net) fall under the same idea. When you're tight, in a big match, or not feeling your best, remove variables.
- **Transition: mush drive → push drop**
  - *Mush drive*: hit the drive with no swing. Paddle starts in front, push forward to opponent's chest, aim high not one inch over the net. Zero variables = 100% make rate. Also easier to time up on fast returns.
  - *Push drop*: after their volley, open the paddle face, lift at a 45-degree angle with no wrist, follow through forward not up. Works on both forehand and backhand. Walks you directly into the kitchen every time.
- **Net game: push/slice, move spots, reach for offense**
  - Replace rolls with push/slice dinks when feeling tight
  - Move the ball around (cross-court, side to side) to force opponent foot movement — many players will pop up
  - Find offense out of the air (reaching/volleying) — *never pull off the bounce* when tight, in big moments, or when the game is close
  - Allow opponents to beat themselves: sometimes dinking a "dead" ball one more time gives your opponent time to make an error rather than forcing a 50/50 ball
- **Personal example:** Richard used this at the US Open final with a broken paddle — played with an unfamiliar paddle and defaulted to pushes and reaches instead of rolls. Won gold.
