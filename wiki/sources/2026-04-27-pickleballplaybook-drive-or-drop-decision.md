# Drive or Drop? Make the Right 3rd Shot Decision 100% of the Time

**Channel:** PickleballPlaybook (Austin Hardy)
**Guest:** Liam Duffin (5.8 rated pro, 3x national / 1x world collegiate champion, Selkirk affiliate, P4 certified coach)
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PIrAY8ByL58
**Date:** 2026-04-27
**Duration:** 20:08

## Key Takeaways

The drive/drop decision is primarily determined by **two factors**: (1) where the opponent is positioned, and (2) where the return lands and at what height. Spin type (slice vs. topspin) adds a third layer.

### The Master Rule
- **Opponent deep / not established → drive**
- **Opponent up and established → drop**

### By Return Depth

**Deep return (you're behind the baseline):**
- Always drive. The kitchen looks tiny from back there — don't put pressure on yourself to hit a reliable drop from distance.
- Drive gets your opponent fast; their body weight going forward makes their volley easier for you to follow up with a 5th-shot drop.
- Driving from deep gives you 1-2 shots before you're at the kitchen. Dropping from deep takes 3+ extra shots.

**Short return (mid-court):**
- Depends entirely on opponent position:
  - Opponent back → drive to keep them deep
  - Opponent established at kitchen → drop (driving is a "death sentence" — they're balanced, ready, and will tee off)
- **One-foot trigger:** If you have at least one foot inside the baseline and can lean forward through contact → you can drop.

### By Return Height (Short Returns)

**Short + high:**
- Can drive even if opponents are established, *only if* the ball is above net tape height — you can hit down on them.
- If the ball is at or below net tape on a short return: you must hit up to clear the net → they smash down → don't drive.

**Short + low:**
- Drop. Driving means hitting up → they hit down at your feet.

**Short + medium (in-between):**
- A "driving drop" at 40-50% pace — advanced shot, mostly 5.0+ territory.

### By Return Spin

**Slice return:**
- Ball stays low, paddle tip toward ground to get beneath it.
- Lean toward **driving** — slice → your drive creates topspin (spin continuation) → ball dips harder at opponent → harder for them to block. Aim higher than normal (even at shoulder level) and it will dip to their feet.
- Dropping a slice is a 1-in-20 shot at most levels (Liam's words). You can, but you'll pop it up under pressure.

**Topspin return:**
- Ball comes in with pace; spin continuation creates underspin for your shot when you hit.
- Lean toward **dropping** — topspin → your drop gets underspin = pillowy, consistent drop. "Way more consistent with a topspin return."
- If one foot is inside the baseline, lean forward through contact and drop.
- Can still drive if opponent is deep or return has extreme pace (they're too far back to establish before your drive lands).

### Pace Adjustment by Court Position

| Position | Drive pace |
|---|---|
| Behind baseline | 70% |
| Mid-court / transition zone | 40-50% |

Lower pace from mid-court = more control, ball still dips at feet. "They already created all the spin, you're just guiding it."

### Opponent Preference Test

At the start of every match, within the first 2-3 points: **test your opponents**. Try some drives. If they smoke them back, switch to drops. Find out which they handle worse and exploit it for the match.
