# The #1 Difference Between Pro and Amateur Pickleball Players (and how to fix it)

**Type:** YouTube instructional
**Date:** 2026-05-18
**Channel:** Cracked Pickleball
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lnAzc-bKMlc
**Duration:** 16:40
**Location:** Pickles at Poipu, Honolulu

## Key Takeaways

- **Watch the opponent's paddle, not the ball** — in the kitchen and transition zone, there is not enough time to track ball flight AND react; paddle position telegraphs shot type: paddle raised above chest = attack coming (lower your paddle to defend feet); paddle neutral = flat/roll coming (stay in ready position); paddle dropped below waist = they must hit up (raise your paddle and prepare to counter); doing the *opposite* of what the opponent's paddle does puts you in the correct position before the ball arrives
- **Respond to position, not panic** — pros treat court position as a scale: low (out of position) = respond with defense; high (dominant position) = respond with offense; amateurs hit panic drives from bad positions trying to force winners; the correct play from bad position is a soft, arcing reset to the middle that buys time to re-enter the point
- **Speed-up at the body, not the middle** — aiming at the open space between two opponents is a beginner mistake; the better target is the right shoulder or right hip ("chicken wing zone") — this forces an awkward pop-up that sets up the second, finishing ball; Tyson McGuffin calls this the one-two punch
- **Third-shot selection by ball height** — simple rule: ball bouncing below the knee → hit a drop; ball above the knee → hit a drive; exceptions apply behind the baseline (a 70% drive to set up a subsequent drop is valid); avoid habit-driven shot selection entirely
- **Mix serves and target backhands** — amateurs repeat the same serve in the same spot; pros evaluate weaknesses every single serve; the golden rule is to aim at the opponent's backhand corner; change serve type (fast/slow/topspin) based on what the returner struggles with
- **Cross the midline** — amateurs protect "their side"; pros follow the ball, stepping into the partner's side when needed; the middle of the court shifts with ball position — defend the court together, not in assigned boxes
