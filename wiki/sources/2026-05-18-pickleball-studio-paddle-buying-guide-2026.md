## Key Takeaways

- **12-question framework** to narrow down paddle choice; split into 5 primary (non-negotiable) and 7 secondary categories
- **5 Primary categories**: (1) Shape — elongated (reach, 2HBH handle), widebody (sweet spot, maneuverability), or hybrid (balance); (2) Swing weight — under 112 for sensitive players, 112–118 average, 118+ for ex-tennis; (3) Power level — control, all-court, or power; (4) Feel — soft/dense vs stiff/hollow spectrum (John Q's 2-axis model); (5) Gen 3 vs Gen 4 durability — polymer honeycomb + foam perimeter vs full foam core
- **7 Secondary categories**: budget ($90–$333, many $100 paddles competitive with $200+), spin (mostly equivalent across paddles; only a few stand out), sweet spot (mostly good now — hard to find a bad one in 2026), thickness (16mm recommended for most, 14mm for specific power/feel needs), handle length (5.25–5.5 in safe range; two-handed backhand needs minimum 5.5 in), grip size (4 1/8 vs 4 1/4 — add overgrip to go bigger, harder to go smaller), and certification (USAPA vs UPAA dual-cert; USAPA is sufficient for 99.99% of players)
- **Gen 3 vs Gen 4 feel**: Gen 3 paddles have a "connected" feel players love, but core crush is the penalty; Gen 4 full-foam eliminates core crush but some find it too dampened; the spectrum of Gen 4 options has widened significantly — stiff/hollow (Boomstick), soft/dense (Prism), and everything between
- **Price reality check**: $100 paddles (Vatic Pro, 11-6-24, Rhombus, Friday, Enhance, Luz) are fully competitive with $200+ options; price is not what limits paddle performance in 2026
