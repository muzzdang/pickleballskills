# Stop Using the Wrong Backhand (Do This Instead)

**Type:** YouTube instructional (Triple C coaching session)
**Date:** 2026-05-22
**Channel:** [Cori Elliott](../entities/cori-elliott.md)
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DAFHOjmTFhI
**Duration:** 6:16

## Key Takeaways

- **Roll vs. flick — clear distinction**: the roll is a shoulder-dominant motion, compact, with minimal wrist; the flick uses actual wrist action through the contact zone; they are used in different situations; the most common error is combining elements of both, creating an unreliable hybrid with no intentional direction
- **Roll mechanics**: drop the paddle downward at an angle, come under the ball, and bring the paddle up — the paddle face stays relatively straight (no closing over the ball); the wrist does NOT pop outward; think of using mostly the paddle motion rather than arm motion forward; cue: "relax your wrist, let it dip, come under it and bring your paddle up"
- **Flick mechanics**: prep with paddle pointing downward at an angle (similar to roll start); as you go forward, the paddle face is *closing* (face turns over the ball, not straight through); stop the paddle in front of you with velocity — it's a "chuck the deck of cards" motion with a sudden deceleration that generates the wrist-snap power; a setup shot primarily, not always a finisher
- **Common errors corrected in this session**: wrist popping outward (on roll — incorrect); not bending knees enough; paddle tip in the wrong position at setup; combining flick and roll randomly without knowing which one they're hitting
- **Body position for flick**: be on your toes with a wide base; core engaged; butt out slightly; reach forward with the non-paddle hand to create balance and help generate shoulder rotation

## Visual Notes

Cori works with a student named Yianni; slow-motion replays during coaching show the wrist path clearly. On the roll: paddle drops, comes under, paddle face stays open, no flip. On the flick: paddle starts low, closes over the ball during contact, paddle stops sharply in front rather than following through.
