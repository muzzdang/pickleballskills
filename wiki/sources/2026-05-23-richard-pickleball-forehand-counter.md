# Master the Forehand Counter in Pickleball: The #1 Skill for Good Hands

**Type:** YouTube instructional
**Date:** 2026-05-23
**Channel:** [Richard Pickleball](../entities/richard-pickleball.md)
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fYD0ta7xn9Y
**Duration:** 10:39

## Key Takeaways

- **The continental grip problem on the forehand counter**: eastern and semi-western grips have a naturally closed paddle face — easy to swing through on a forehand counter; continental grip opens the face, which causes the typical wrist-slap problem where players try to time a snap and lose control
- **Ping-pong forehand model**: the fix is a unit-turn shoulder motion like a ping-pong forehand — bring the paddle back (not just the wrist), turn the shoulder, then accelerate with the arm while the wrist stays *preset and locked*; Ben Johns is cited as doing this well; the motion is stiff-feeling compared to wrist-based approaches but is far more consistent
- **Wrist preset**: as soon as the paddle comes back, the wrist closes slightly (as if pointing a finger down and out); it does NOT close during the swing; cue: "first set, move, hit" — never set and move at the same time; this preset position allows a single clean arm acceleration regardless of ball height
- **Advanced: paddle-tip-drop for different heights**: closing the wrist pre-contact also drops the paddle tip slightly; now the tip tracks ball height — tip up for high balls, tip sideways for middle, tip down for low balls; this single wrist preset covers all heights with the same swing path, which is otherwise impossible with a neutral-wrist continental grip
- **Why this matters in continental**: on the left (backhand) side players can be slightly late and still control the redirect; on the forehand side in continental, being even a little late sends the ball wide — the preset removes that timing sensitivity
- **Common mistake**: the "slappy wrist" forehand counter — wrist stays open, player tries to time a snap at contact; works occasionally at slow speeds but breaks down at fast pace; many players plateau here for years

## Visual Notes

Richard shows the wrist preset position from the camera's perspective: paddle comes back, wrist turns so the palm faces slightly downward (not outward), creating a closed face before the swing. Multiple reps at different heights show the paddle tip staying level with the incoming ball across all three zones (high/mid/low). The drill shown — partner hits dead balls at varied heights, Richard counters — is immediately applicable.
